FREEDOM Act vs. CERTAIN Act

A deep analysis of two competing approaches to permitting certainty — what they do, how they differ, where they fail, and what would actually work.

Two bipartisan House bills are competing to define the next generation of federal permitting reform. Both respond to the same crisis: an administration that has weaponized the permitting process to block projects it disfavors, creating unprecedented uncertainty for energy developers of all kinds. But they take meaningfully different approaches. This analysis goes provision-by-provision through both, red-teams them aggressively from multiple directions, and synthesizes a path forward.

I. The Problem These Bills Are Trying to Solve

Before examining the legislation, we need to understand the specific crisis that produced it. The problem isn't merely "permitting takes too long" — the decades-old complaint — but something newer and more destabilizing: the executive branch has discovered it can effectively kill permitted projects by revoking, suspending, or indefinitely stalling authorizations after they've been granted.

The Burgum Memo: A Permitting Moratorium by Another Name

On July 15, 2025, Interior Secretary Doug Burgum issued a memorandum requiring that every decision, action, consultation, and undertaking related to wind and solar energy be elevated through a three-tier review process culminating in personal approval by the Secretary. The memo identified 69 categories of activities now requiring Secretary-level sign-off, including routine items like temporary use permits and access road authorizations.

Scale of Impact

According to SEIA, the memo created a "near complete moratorium on permitting for any project in which the Department of Interior may play a role, on both federal and private land, no matter how minor."

  • 500+ projects in the development pipeline face delays or cancellation
  • 116 GW of capacity at risk — approximately half of all planned power capacity additions through 2030
  • 18 states have over 50% of their planned electricity capacity at risk
  • 27 of the 69 activities affect projects on private land

A follow-up directive on July 29 (Secretarial Order 3437) went further, directing DOI to eliminate "preferential treatment" toward solar and wind, and instructing the DOI Solicitor to review pending litigation to identify cases for voluntary remand — meaning the government would actively seek to undo its own past approvals.

On August 20, 2025, the President stated plainly: “We’re not going to approve solar or wind.”

The Collapse of CEQ as a Regulatory Framework

The Council on Environmental Quality (CEQ) has been the primary rulemaking body for NEPA implementation since 1978. But its authority has been demolished from two directions simultaneously:

  1. Judicial: The D.C. Circuit (November 2024) and a North Dakota federal court (February 2025) both found that CEQ likely never had statutory authority to issue binding NEPA regulations — not in 2024, not in 2020, and arguably not in 1978.
  2. Executive: On January 8, 2026, CEQ published a final rule rescinding all NEPA implementing regulations from 40 C.F.R. Parts 1500–1508.

The result: each federal agency now implements NEPA under its own rules, creating divergent interpretations and increased complexity. As one law firm titled their analysis: “The CEQ Has No Clothes.”

Why This Matters for These Bills

This is the core insight that separates these two bills from previous permitting reform efforts: the problem is no longer just speed — it's certainty. A developer who spends years navigating the permitting process and millions of dollars in compliance can have everything yanked away by a new administration that simply doesn't like their project type. No prior permitting reform legislation adequately addressed this.

Core Architecture: 11 Sections

The CERTAIN Act is a 16-page discussion draft organized into 11 substantive sections. Its theory of the case is procedural: build a comprehensive end-to-end permitting framework with clear rules that constrain agency discretion at every stage.

Section (a) — Certainty (The Headline Provision)

The anchor provision. A federal agency may not revoke, rescind, withdraw, terminate, suspend, amend, alter, or take any other action to interfere with an authorization unless one of five narrow exceptions applies:

  1. Required by order of a court of competent jurisdiction
  2. The holder materially breached the authorization terms or violated applicable law
  3. The authorization was obtained through fraud, intentional concealment, or material misrepresentation
  4. Action is necessary to prevent specific, immediate, substantial, and proximate harm to life, property, national security, or defense not considered in the underlying environmental review
  5. The holder or project sponsor themselves requested the action

Evidentiary standard: Any revocation action must be “supported by clear and convincing evidence and reasonably limited in duration and scope.”

Anti-vacatur provision: No federal agency may petition a court for vacatur or voluntary remand of an authorization unless the holder consents in writing.

Section (b) — Notice

Before any revocation action, the agency must notify the authorization holder in writing with a detailed explanation, the statutory authority relied upon, and supporting evidence.

Section (c) — Timelines and Process

Establishes strict deadlines for the application-to-determination pipeline:

StageDeadline
Acknowledge receipt & determine completeness60 days (30 for pending applications)
Respond to resubmissions30 days
Issue Notice of Intent for NEPA review60 days after deeming complete
Automatic “deemed complete” backstop6 months if agency hasn't acted
Rolling backstop (after written attestation)90 days, then every 30 days

Critically: application completeness determinations may not be revoked, and agencies may not identify new required information after the initial exchange (unless necessary for statutory obligations). This directly addresses the tactic of endlessly requesting supplemental information to stall projects.

Section (d) — Preventing Unnecessary Delays (CEQ Mediation)

This is the provision that draws the most criticism. CEQ is empowered to:

  • Issue guidance to facilitate timely environmental reviews
  • Mediate disputes between stakeholders; if unresolved within 30 days, CEQ can provide specific direction to resolve the dispute within 60 days
  • CEQ mediation actions are not subject to judicial review
  • Submit annual reports to Congress on disputes and resolution times

Section (e) — Judicial Review

  • Review in the U.S. Court of Appeals for the circuit where the project is located
  • Missed deadlines create a rebuttable presumption of unreasonable delay
  • Courts must issue decisions within 180 days
  • If delay is found unreasonable, the court shall set a specific deadline

Section (f) — Denial of Authorizations

Agencies may not deny authorizations without a written determination supported by the record and authorized by statute. Denials are final agency actions subject to judicial review.

Section (g) — Process Coordination

  • Lead agency identified within 30 days of application completeness
  • Participating agencies identified within 60 days
  • Federal agencies must confirm participation within 30 days
  • Non-federal agencies that fail to confirm within 30 days waive jurisdiction over the federal review
  • All reviews must be concurrent producing a single environmental document
  • Coordination plan within 90 days with categorization of authorizations as “routine” or “complex”

Section (h) — Deadlines for Federal Authorizations

  • 90 days after publishing final environmental document for complex authorizations
  • 30 days for routine authorizations

Section (i) — Accountability

When an agency misses a deadline: immediate notification to the public, project sponsor, CEQ, and relevant Secretaries. Within 30 days: updated schedule not exceeding 6 months. The project sponsor gets administrative review by a senior career agency official within 90 days.

Section (j) — Definitions

Key terms: “Authorization” covers any permit, approval, finding, certification, or consent required under federal law (excluding Atomic Energy Act). “Complex authorization” requires an environmental document, ESA Section 7, NHPA Section 106, new ROW/easement/lease, or CWA Section 404 permit.

Section (k) — Federal Permitting Capacity

  • Every 5 years, agencies must report on permitting capacity by state/region
  • OPM must develop plans to increase personnel where shortfalls exist
  • Agencies receive direct hire authority for permitting positions
  • Authorization of appropriations: “such sums as is necessary”

The FREEDOM Act is a 78-page formally introduced bill (referred to five House committees). It creates a new Title XII in the Energy Act of 2020. Its theory of the case is more muscular: create enforceable rights for project sponsors, backed by financial penalties, insurance mechanisms, and judicial enforcement.

Core Architecture: 7 Subtitles

Subtitle A — Definitions

Establishes 11 core terms including “covered energy project” (energy infrastructure, carbon capture, mineral extraction), “authorization” (permits and consultations), “lead agency,” and “complex authorization.”

Subtitle B — Federal Land Energy and Mineral Reforms

  • Cost recovery agreements within 30 days of complete applications
  • Mandatory categorical exclusions for low-disturbance activities (under 5 acres with prior analysis)
  • Activities on previously disturbed land analyzed in prior environmental documents can proceed

Subtitle C — The De-Risking Compensation Program

The most innovative — and most controversial — provision. An insurance-like program administered by DOE:

How the De-Risking Fund Works

  • Annual premiums: 1.5% of “capital contribution” (expenditures and binding commitments, excluding executive pay and overhead)
  • Minimum threshold: $5 million capital contribution to be eligible
  • Exclusive jurisdiction: Court of Federal Claims
  • Triggers: Permit revocation, missed agency deadlines, project cancellation due to agency inaction, or government “running out the clock” rendering a project commercially unviable
  • Maximum payout: Equal to the company’s capital contribution (no profit recovery)
  • Opt-in: Participation is voluntary

Subtitle D — Permitting Timelines and Performance Fund

Authorization TypeDeadline
Routine authorizations90 days
Complex authorizations1 year
Complex + EIS required2 years
Extensions (sponsor-requested)Up to 90 additional days
  • Creates a $50 million Permitting Performance Fund from civil penalties and appropriations
  • When agencies miss deadlines, project sponsors may petition a court to hire court-approved contractors to complete environmental documentation at no cost to the project

Subtitle E — Expedited Judicial Review and Enforcement

  • Project sponsors may petition in the D.C. Circuit, the circuit where the project is located, or the applicable district court
  • 120-day decision deadline for courts
  • “Limited vacatur” standard: Courts can only vacate permits if there is “specific, imminent, and irreparable environmental harm”
  • Restricts non-project sponsors from extending legal challenges
  • Attorney fees and costs awarded when unreasonable delay is proven

Subtitle F — Limiting Orders Against Fully Permitted Projects

The Stop-Work Protection

Federal agencies are prohibited from halting projects that have a “substantial majority” of authorizations unless:

  1. Clear, immediate, substantiated harm exists with no viable alternatives, OR
  2. The authorization is illegal and halting is the only available remedy

Agencies cannot petition for voluntary remand without project sponsor consent. Violations trigger “just compensation.”

Subtitle G — GAO Reporting

  • GAO must survey industry satisfaction annually
  • Reports at 180 days and annually on: deadline compliance, unfair treatment allegations, staffing levels, permitting costs, and equal treatment across energy technologies

Additional Provisions

  • Geothermal reforms: Annual lease sales (not biennial), concurrent permitting for exploration/drilling/production, a “Geothermal Ombudsman” at BLM
  • Non-federal land provisions: No federal drilling permit required where the government owns <50% of minerals and doesn't own/lease the surface
  • Civil penalties: $1,000 to $100,000 per day for agency noncompliance, paid from agency general management accounts to the project sponsor
  • Effective date: 90 days for new projects; 120 days for pending projects with retroactive coverage

Theory of the Case: Two Different Philosophies

CERTAIN Act Philosophy

“Build the rails, then make the train run on them.”

The CERTAIN Act believes the problem is structural: there's no comprehensive end-to-end framework governing how permits move from application to final determination. Fix the process itself — with clear milestones, concurrent review, agency coordination, staffing mandates, and a CEQ mediation backstop — and the outcomes will follow. The permit-certainty provision is the shield; the process framework is the engine.

This approach is more consensus-friendly and draws from the Problem Solvers Caucus tradition of both parties finding procedural middle ground. Environmental groups can accept it because it doesn't weaken environmental review — it just makes it faster and more predictable.

FREEDOM Act Philosophy

“Give project sponsors weapons they can actually use.”

The FREEDOM Act believes the problem is power asymmetry: agencies face no consequences for delay, obstruction, or revocation, while developers absorb all the cost. Fix the incentives — with financial penalties, insurance payouts, contractor substitution, expedited courts, and damages for stop-work violations — and agencies will behave. The permit-certainty provision is one of many teeth.

This approach is more confrontational and reflects the lesson of the offshore wind suspensions: mere process reforms don't help when an administration decides to weaponize its authority. You need enforceable rights, financial accountability, and judicial muscle.

V. Red Team Analysis

Both bills are going to be attacked from two opposite directions. The question is which attacks are valid.

Red Team: Too Pro-Industry?

Attacks on the FREEDOM Act

  1. The “limited vacatur” standard guts judicial oversight. By requiring “specific, imminent, and irreparable environmental harm” to vacate a permit, the bill makes it nearly impossible for courts to correct agency errors in environmental review. An agency could approve a deeply flawed EIS, and the courts couldn't undo the permit unless the project was about to cause immediate, provable damage. This is a massive departure from existing APA review.
  2. Categorical exclusions for 5-acre sites are a loophole. Five acres of previously disturbed land might seem small, but projects can be segmented. A developer could break a large project into sub-5-acre parcels on “previously disturbed” land to avoid NEPA entirely.
  3. The prohibition on voluntary remand ties agencies' hands. If an agency discovers a genuine error in its own environmental review, it should be able to fix it. Requiring the project sponsor's consent to seek remand means errors can't be corrected when correction would inconvenience the developer.
  4. Civil penalties from agency budgets punish the wrong people. Taking $100K/day from an agency's general management account doesn't punish the political appointee who ordered the delay — it punishes the career staff by reducing resources for all agency operations, including permitting. This is a recipe for degrading exactly the capacity the bill claims to want to build.
  5. The contractor substitution mechanism is an end-run around public expertise. Allowing project sponsors to hire private contractors to complete environmental documentation when agencies miss deadlines creates a structural conflict of interest: the entity being reviewed selects the entity conducting the review.

Attacks on the CERTAIN Act

  1. The “deemed complete” backstop is dangerous. If an agency hasn't determined completeness within 6 months, the application is automatically deemed complete. But what if the application is genuinely, substantively incomplete? What if a developer submits a 10-page EIS application for a 2 GW project? The backstop could force agencies to process woefully inadequate applications.
  2. The waiver of jurisdiction for non-participating agencies undermines environmental review. If a consulting agency like the Fish and Wildlife Service fails to confirm participation within 30 days, it “waives jurisdiction.” This means an ESA-listed species could be affected by a project and the expert agency simply doesn't participate because it missed a bureaucratic deadline.
  3. The “no new information” rule after initial completeness is rigid. Environmental conditions change. New species are listed. New contamination is discovered. The prohibition on agencies identifying new required information after the initial exchange could prevent consideration of genuinely important new environmental factors.

Red Team: Too Weak?

Attacks on the CERTAIN Act

  1. No financial consequences for agency non-compliance. The CERTAIN Act's accountability section (i) requires notification and an updated schedule when deadlines are missed — but there's no penalty. An agency can miss every deadline, send a notification letter, set a new schedule, miss that too, and the only recourse is administrative review by a “senior career official” at the same agency. This is circular.
  2. CEQ mediation is toothless because CEQ is part of the problem. The bill gives CEQ power to mediate disputes and “provide specific direction.” But CEQ answers to the President. If the President's policy is to block solar and wind, CEQ's “mediation” will align with that policy. Making this mediation non-judicially-reviewable actually makes it worse: you can't even challenge CEQ's biased mediation in court.
  3. The 180-day court deadline is too slow. Six months for a judicial decision, when developers are hemorrhaging millions per month in carrying costs, is too long. And it's merely a target — courts aren't bound by it.
  4. No compensation for losses from delay or revocation. A developer who loses a billion-dollar project to an unlawful revocation can go to court and get the revocation reversed — but they can't recover any of the losses incurred during the delay. The CERTAIN Act provides no financial remedy for government-caused harm.
  5. “Such sums as is necessary” is a promise that won't be kept. The agency capacity provision authorizes appropriations but doesn't mandate them. Congress routinely ignores authorizations. Without a dedicated funding stream (like the FREEDOM Act's Performance Fund), the staffing provisions are aspirational.

Attacks on the FREEDOM Act

  1. The “substantial majority” threshold is undefined. Subtitle F protects projects with a “substantial majority of authorizations.” What's a substantial majority? 60%? 80%? This invites litigation over the threshold itself. An administration could argue that a project with 95% of permits but missing one critical authorization isn't “fully permitted.”
  2. The de-risking fund only covers capital contribution, not opportunity cost or financing. If a developer invested $200M and the project would have generated $2B in revenue over 20 years, the maximum recovery is $200M. The real economic harm — the lost value of the project — is uncompensated.
  3. The $5M minimum entry excludes small developers. Community energy projects, small municipal utilities, and individual landowners who might need protection the most are excluded from the insurance program.
  4. Court-approved contractors can't compel agency cooperation. Even if a contractor is appointed to complete environmental documentation, they need agency data, models, and expertise. An uncooperative agency can simply refuse to provide supporting information, rendering the contractor useless.

Red Team: How Could These Laws Be Abused?

Abuse Scenarios

Under the CERTAIN Act:

  • Deemed-complete gaming: A bad-faith developer could submit a deliberately thin application, stall through the response cycle, and trigger the 6-month automatic deemed-complete backstop — then argue that the agency is obligated to process the application as-is. This creates pressure to approve undercooked projects.
  • Jurisdiction-waiver exploitation: A developer could structure timelines to ensure that the Fish and Wildlife Service or other consulting agencies receive their participation invitations during peak workload periods, miss the 30-day response window, and thereby “waive” their jurisdiction over the project. This could be used to avoid ESA or other environmental consultations.

Under the FREEDOM Act:

  • Insurance arbitrage: A developer with a questionable project could pay the 1.5% premium, invest minimally, then pursue an aggressive legal strategy to provoke a permit denial or delay — collecting insurance proceeds that exceed what the project would have returned on its own.
  • Categorical exclusion segmentation: Breaking large projects into sub-5-acre parcels on “previously disturbed” land to systematically avoid NEPA review for what is effectively a large-scale project.
  • Penalty weaponization: In a complex multi-agency review, a project sponsor could deliberately create conditions that make deadline compliance impossible (e.g., submitting incomplete supplemental materials late, then claiming the agency missed its deadline) to trigger $100K/day penalties.

Red Team: How a Hostile Administration Could Still Block Projects

This is perhaps the most important red team exercise. If a future administration wants to block projects despite these laws, what are the pathways?

Evasion Playbook: Against the CERTAIN Act

  1. Exploit the “specific, immediate, substantial, and proximate harm” exception. An administration could manufacture national security or public safety concerns (as with the offshore wind radar claims) to justify revocations under exception (D). The “clear and convincing evidence” standard helps, but a sufficiently creative agency can produce supporting documentation. The burden falls on the developer to prove in court that the evidence is insufficient.
  2. Weaponize CEQ mediation. Since CEQ mediation is not judicially reviewable, a hostile CEQ could “mediate” disputes by consistently siding with the obstructing agency, issuing “specific direction” that further delays projects. There's no appeal.
  3. Slow-walk with written attestations. After 6 months, the deemed-complete backstop kicks in — unless the agency sends a “written attestation that the sponsor failed to respond or provided materially deficient information.” An agency could send endlessly nitpicking attestations every 30 days to prevent the backstop from triggering.
  4. Deny rather than revoke. The certainty provision protects issued permits. It doesn't prevent an agency from simply never approving an application in the first place. An agency could take every application through the full timeline, then deny it with a “written determination supported by the record.” Denials are reviewable, but proving they were politically motivated (rather than substantively justified) is difficult.
  5. Defund permitting staff. If the administration doesn't request permitting appropriations and Congress doesn't mandate them (the bill says “such sums as is necessary”), agencies literally can't process applications — not because they're obstructing, but because they lack staff. The accountability provisions require only that the agency report the delay and set a new schedule.

Evasion Playbook: Against the FREEDOM Act

  1. Challenge “covered energy project” definitions. The bill applies to “energy infrastructure development, carbon capture, or mineral extraction.” An agency could argue that a specific project doesn't qualify — e.g., that a transmission line isn't “energy infrastructure development” or that a battery storage facility isn't covered.
  2. Litigate “substantial majority” endlessly. Since the threshold for Subtitle F protection is undefined, an administration could challenge whether any given project has a “substantial majority” of authorizations, creating years of definitional litigation.
  3. Make contractors ineffective. When court-approved contractors are appointed to complete environmental work, the agency could refuse to provide data, models, or institutional knowledge, making the contractor unable to complete the work competently. The bill doesn't compel agency cooperation with contractors.
  4. Accept penalties as the cost of obstruction. $100K/day sounds significant, but for a determined administration blocking a politically disfavored $10 billion project, it's rounding error. And if the penalties come from agency budgets (not political appointees' personal liability), the political cost is indirect.
  5. Exploit the “clear, immediate, substantiated harm” exception. Like the CERTAIN Act's exception (D), the FREEDOM Act allows stop-work orders where harm exists “with no viable alternatives.” A creative agency could manufacture harm scenarios and argue there's no alternative to halting the project.
  6. Deny insurance claims aggressively. DOE administers the de-risking fund. Under a hostile administration, DOE could interpret the compensation triggers narrowly, deny claims, and force developers into the Court of Federal Claims — adding years and millions in legal costs.

VI. Effectiveness Audit: Do They Solve the Stated Problems?

The Stated Problems

Both bills identify the same core problems, stated slightly differently:

  1. Permits are being revoked or suspended for political reasons after developers have invested billions in compliance and construction
  2. Agency timelines are unpredictable and unenforceable — developers face years-long delays with no recourse
  3. The permitting process changes with each administration — there's no durable, statutory framework
  4. Developers bear all the risk of government delay, obstruction, or reversal, with no compensation
  5. The current process is not tech-neutral — administrations favor some energy types over others

Overall Assessment

The CERTAIN Act is better at process architecture: comprehensive timelines, concurrent review mandates, agency coordination, staffing capacity, and preventing agencies from creating delays through procedural obfuscation. It builds the machine.

The FREEDOM Act is better at enforcement and remedies: financial penalties, insurance compensation, contractor substitution, expedited judicial review, and stop-work protections with teeth. It gives project sponsors power.

Neither bill alone is sufficient. The CERTAIN Act builds a good process but provides no consequences when the process is violated. The FREEDOM Act provides strong consequences but has a less comprehensive process framework.

VII. Named Critiques: Are They Valid?

“The de-risking fund will only help bad projects, not good ones”

The critique: Good projects with strong economics don't need insurance — they'll fight through delays on the strength of their business case. The projects that will rely on the fund are marginal projects that wouldn't be viable without the insurance backstop. The fund therefore subsidizes weak projects.

Validity: Partially valid, but mostly wrong.

The critique misunderstands the risk profile. The projects most harmed by government-caused delay are capital-intensive, long-lead-time projects — exactly the projects the country most needs (offshore wind, large-scale transmission, nuclear, carbon capture). These aren't “bad” projects; they're projects where a 2-year delay destroys the financing structure. A 2 GW offshore wind project with $12 billion in investment isn't marginal — but its financing terms assume a specific construction timeline, and government-caused delay can trigger loan covenants, increase carrying costs, and cause investor withdrawals.

That said, there's a real concern about moral hazard. A 1.5% premium with a maximum payout equal to capital contribution creates a scenario where a developer with a questionable project could invest $100M, pay $1.5M/year in premiums, and if the project fails due to a (possibly predictable) permit denial, recover the full $100M. The fund needs stronger underwriting criteria or risk-adjusted premiums.

Synthesis

The de-risking fund is a genuinely good idea that needs better design. Risk-adjusted premiums (not a flat 1.5%), an independent board (not DOE), and exclusions for projects where permit risk was foreseeable at time of enrollment would address the moral hazard concern without eliminating the mechanism.

“The FREEDOM Act defunds agencies”

The critique: By imposing $1K–$100K/day penalties from agency general management accounts, the bill effectively punishes agencies for being understaffed by taking away more resources. This creates a death spiral: agencies miss deadlines because they lack staff, then lose budget to penalties, which makes them even less able to process permits.

Validity: Substantially valid.

This is a real design flaw. The political appointee who orders an agency to stall a disfavored project doesn't personally pay the penalty — the career staff do, through reduced budgets. And in cases where delays are caused by genuine understaffing (not political obstruction), the penalties are perverse.

The FREEDOM Act partially addresses this with the $50M Permitting Performance Fund, but the fund is small relative to the penalty exposure. If even five major projects trigger penalties simultaneously, the penalties could exceed the Performance Fund.

Synthesis

Penalties should be directed at the decision-maker, not the agency budget. Options: personal liability for political appointees who order unlawful delays; mandatory IG investigations when penalties are triggered; penalties directed to a segregated permitting fund rather than project sponsors. The CERTAIN Act's approach — direct hire authority + capacity reporting — is more constructive for the understaffing problem.

“You can't trust CEQ — they're part of the administration”

The critique: The CERTAIN Act gives CEQ a central mediation role and exempts that mediation from judicial review. But CEQ answers to the President. If the President's policy is to block solar and wind projects, CEQ's “mediation” will align with that policy — and there's no way to challenge it in court.

Validity: Substantially valid.

The empirical evidence is devastating. CEQ regulations have whipsawed with every administration since 2020:

  • 1978 (Carter): CEQ issues binding NEPA regs — stable for 42 years
  • 2020 (Trump 1.0): Substantially revised
  • 2021, 2024 (Biden): Revised again
  • 2025 (Trump 2.0): Rescinded entirely

And two federal courts have found CEQ likely never had binding regulatory authority at all. To give this entity a central mediation role in a bill designed to provide permitting certainty is, at best, ironic.

Worse, making CEQ mediation non-judicially-reviewable converts a bug into a feature — for the administration. A hostile CEQ can issue binding “specific direction” that kills a project, and the developer can't challenge it in court.

Synthesis

The mediation function should be assigned to an entity with structural independence from the White House. Options: (1) the Federal Permitting Improvement Steering Council (FPISC), which already exists and has a statutory basis; (2) an independent permitting ombudsman housed within GAO or the judiciary; (3) a bipartisan permitting commission modeled on FERC. If CEQ retains any role, its mediation must be subject to judicial review.

“The CERTAIN Act's remedies are too weak”

The critique: The CERTAIN Act has good procedural architecture but no enforcement mechanism with teeth. When an agency violates the bill's requirements, the consequences are: notification, a new schedule, and administrative review by a senior career official at the same agency. This is asking the offender to grade its own homework.

Validity: Fully valid.

Compare the remedies:

The CERTAIN Act puts the burden of enforcement on the developer: go to court (at your expense), prove unreasonable delay, and hope the court sets a deadline the agency will actually follow. The FREEDOM Act puts the burden on the agency: miss a deadline and you start hemorrhaging money.

Synthesis

The CERTAIN Act needs enforcement teeth. Importing a version of the FREEDOM Act's graduated penalty structure would address this directly. But the FREEDOM Act's approach also needs refinement — penalties from agency budgets should be reformed as discussed above.

VIII. Synthesis: The Best Path Forward

Having red-teamed both bills aggressively, the synthesis is clear: neither bill alone is sufficient, but their strengths are complementary. The CERTAIN Act's process architecture plus the FREEDOM Act's enforcement mechanisms, with corrections to each bill's weaknesses, would produce the strongest possible permitting certainty law.

Principles for a Merged Bill

  1. Permit certainty must be bipartisan and durable. The tech-neutral approach in both bills is essential and must be non-negotiable. Any carveout (like the SPEED Act's offshore wind exclusion) will immediately fracture the coalition. Both parties must be willing to protect projects they don't like to protect projects they do like.
  2. Process reforms and enforcement mechanisms are both necessary. Process without enforcement produces the CERTAIN Act's weakness: a well-designed system with no consequences for violation. Enforcement without process produces the FREEDOM Act's weakness: penalties that don't fix the underlying dysfunction.
  3. The executive branch cannot be trusted to regulate itself. CEQ, DOE, and DOI have all demonstrated they will follow presidential direction regardless of statutory mandates. Any mediation, insurance, or oversight function must be structurally independent or subject to judicial review.
  4. Agency capacity must be built, not depleted. Penalties that reduce agency budgets are counterproductive. The goal is to make agencies able to process permits, not to punish them for lacking the resources to do so.
  5. Financial remedies must be available but carefully designed. The de-risking fund concept is sound but needs risk-adjusted premiums, independent administration, and anti-abuse safeguards. Small developers need access too.

Model Bill: What Would Work

Title I — Permit Certainty (from CERTAIN Act Section (a), strengthened)

  • Adopt the CERTAIN Act's five narrow revocation exceptions with clear-and-convincing evidence standard
  • Add the FREEDOM Act's anti-voluntary-remand provision
  • Add the FREEDOM Act's “just compensation” provision for unlawful revocations
  • Fix: Define “substantial majority of authorizations” explicitly (e.g., “all complex authorizations and 75% or more of routine authorizations”)
  • Fix: Include retroactive protection for projects unlawfully suspended since January 2025

Title II — Process Framework (from CERTAIN Act Sections (b)–(c), (f)–(h))

  • Adopt the CERTAIN Act's comprehensive application-to-determination pipeline
  • Adopt the deemed-complete backstop (6 months) but fix it: require the agency to demonstrate the application is substantively incomplete (not just that it hasn't gotten around to reviewing it)
  • Adopt concurrent review, lead agency designation, and single environmental document requirements
  • Fix: Modify the jurisdiction-waiver for non-participating agencies to allow a 60-day window (not 30) and provide an exemption for ESA Section 7 consultations (species protection shouldn't be waived by bureaucratic delay)
  • Adopt the FREEDOM Act's binding deadlines: 90 days / 1 year / 2 years by complexity tier

Title III — Enforcement (adapted from FREEDOM Act Subtitles D–E)

  • Graduated financial penalties for missed deadlines, but directed to a segregated Permitting Capacity Fund (not paid from agency general budgets or to project sponsors)
  • Mandatory Inspector General investigation when cumulative penalties exceed $500K for any single project
  • Court-approved contractor substitution when deadlines are missed, with a statutory duty of cooperation requiring agencies to provide data and models to contractors
  • Expedited judicial review: 120-day court deadline (FREEDOM Act) in the circuit where the project is located (CERTAIN Act)
  • Attorney fees and costs for successful unreasonable-delay claims

Title IV — De-Risking (adapted from FREEDOM Act Subtitle C)

  • Insurance-like compensation fund, but administered by an independent board (not DOE)
  • Risk-adjusted premiums (not flat 1.5%) based on project complexity, prior agency track record for that project type, and geographic risk
  • Lower the $5M threshold to $1M to include smaller community energy projects
  • Require actuarial review every 3 years
  • Exclude projects where permit risk was foreseeable and documented at time of enrollment (anti-moral-hazard)

Title V — Agency Capacity (from CERTAIN Act Section (k), expanded)

  • Adopt the CERTAIN Act's capacity reporting and direct hire authority
  • Create a dedicated Permitting Capacity Fund (seeded with $200M, replenished by enforcement penalties and a small fee on permit applications)
  • Fund competitive federal permitting salaries to reduce agency brain drain to consulting firms
  • Mandate that appropriations committees consider agency capacity reports when setting budgets

Title VI — Dispute Resolution (replacing CERTAIN Act Section (d))

  • Replace CEQ mediation with FPISC (Federal Permitting Improvement Steering Council) as the mediation body
  • FPISC already has statutory authority and bipartisan support; strengthen it with dedicated funding and staff
  • Make mediation outcomes subject to judicial review (unlike the CERTAIN Act's non-reviewable CEQ role)
  • Add a Congressional notification trigger: if mediation fails twice for the same project, the relevant congressional committees receive automatic notification

Title VII — Accountability and Transparency (merged from both)

  • GAO annual survey of industry satisfaction (FREEDOM Act)
  • Agency deadline compliance reporting (both)
  • Equal-treatment-across-technologies audit (FREEDOM Act)
  • Capacity reporting (CERTAIN Act)
  • Public permitting dashboard showing real-time status of all covered projects

IX. How This Would Work in Practice

Worked Scenarios

Scenario 1: A future administration tries to replicate the Burgum Memo

Day 1

New Interior Secretary issues memorandum requiring elevated review for all solar/wind permits, centralizing authority in the Secretary's office.

Under Current Law

The memo takes effect immediately. Permits grind to a halt. Developers file lawsuits, which take 1–3 years. Hundreds of projects stall. Billions in investment are frozen.

Under the Merged Bill

Title I — All issued permits are immediately protected. The Secretary cannot revoke, suspend, or alter them without clear and convincing evidence of one of five narrow exceptions. Any attempt triggers written notice requirements and immediate judicial review.

Title II — Pending applications continue under binding statutory deadlines regardless of the memo. The memo cannot override the 60-day completeness determination, the 6-month deemed-complete backstop, or the 90-day/1-year/2-year authorization deadlines.

Title III — If the memo causes agencies to miss deadlines, graduated penalties begin accruing to the Permitting Capacity Fund. Project sponsors can petition for court-approved contractors. An IG investigation is automatically triggered when penalties exceed $500K.

Title VI — FPISC mediates disputes between the Secretary's office and project sponsors. If mediation fails twice, congressional committees are automatically notified.

Scenario 2: An administration suspends all five offshore wind projects under construction

Day 1

BOEM issues suspension letters citing national security concerns about radar interference.

Under Current Law

Developers file emergency motions. Courts eventually strike down the orders (as happened in December 2025–January 2026), but months of delay cost billions. No compensation.

Under the Merged Bill

Title I — The suspension is presumptively unlawful because the projects have a “substantial majority of authorizations” (defined by the bill). The only permitted exception requires clear, immediate, substantiated harm with no viable alternatives. The radar claim fails this test (it was “shown to be quite baseless” by experts).

Title III — Developers file in federal court with a 120-day decision deadline. Attorney fees and costs are awarded when the suspension is struck down.

Title I — “Just compensation” is owed for the period of unlawful suspension. Given Equinor's reported ~$1B cost from the earlier Empire Wind suspension, this creates a powerful deterrent.

Title IV — Developers who enrolled in the de-risking fund can file claims for losses during the suspension period, providing immediate financial relief while litigation proceeds.

Scenario 3: An agency slow-walks a solar project by endlessly requesting supplemental information

Under the Merged Bill

Title II — The agency has 60 days to determine application completeness. It must specify all needed supplemental information in one comprehensive request. After the developer responds, the agency cannot request new information (except for statutory obligations). If the agency doesn't act within 6 months, the application is automatically deemed complete (the agency can prevent this only by demonstrating the application is substantively incomplete, not merely that it hasn't been reviewed).

Title III — Once deadlines are triggered, each missed milestone accrues penalties and entitles the developer to contractor substitution.

Result: The tactic of endless supplemental information requests — the most common form of permit obstruction — is directly neutralized.

Scenario 4: A future Democratic administration wants to block a pipeline project

Under the Merged Bill

The tech-neutral provisions apply equally. A future administration cannot:

  • Revoke an issued pipeline permit without satisfying the 5-exception framework with clear and convincing evidence
  • Slow-walk a pipeline application beyond the statutory deadlines without triggering penalties
  • Issue a stop-work order on a pipeline under construction without meeting the “clear, immediate harm, no viable alternatives” standard
  • Use CEQ/FPISC mediation to stealthily block the project (because mediation is judicially reviewable)

This is the bipartisan durability test: the law must constrain both a pro-fossil and a pro-renewable administration equally. Tech neutrality is what makes the coalition possible.

The Core Insight

The most important feature of this synthesis is that it converts permitting from a discretionary executive function into a statutory rights framework. Developers don't need to hope for a friendly administration — they have enforceable legal rights backed by financial remedies. And the public doesn't lose environmental protection — the environmental review still happens, it just happens on a schedule, with accountability, and the results can't be arbitrarily reversed.

This is not pro-industry or anti-industry. It is anti-arbitrary. It is the government keeping its word.

X. Beyond Current Bills: How to Make This Maximally Strong

Both the FREEDOM and CERTAIN Acts operate within the traditional framework: you need a permit, you apply for it, the government reviews it, and then you hope it sticks. But the most powerful reforms don’t just speed up the existing process — they change the underlying architecture. What follows is a toolkit of proven legal mechanisms from other domains, each of which could be imported into permitting reform to create a fundamentally stronger regime.

1. Shift the Burden of Proof to the Government

The single most impactful structural change. Currently, when a permit is challenged or revoked, the developer bears most of the practical burden: they must go to court, prove the revocation was arbitrary, and absorb all costs during the fight. The government can revoke first and justify later. Flipping this would transform the power dynamic.

Presumption of Validity (Patent Law Model)

35 U.S.C. §282 provides: “A patent shall be presumed valid. The burden of establishing invalidity… shall rest on the party asserting such invalidity.” The Supreme Court in Microsoft v. i4i (2011) confirmed this requires clear and convincing evidence — not merely a preponderance.

Translation to Permitting

A reform statute could provide: “A permit issued under this chapter shall be presumed valid. The burden of establishing grounds for revocation, suspension, or modification shall rest on the agency asserting such grounds, which must be proven by clear and convincing evidence.”

The CERTAIN Act partially adopts this with its “clear and convincing evidence” standard, but doesn't create the affirmative presumption of validity. Adding the presumption changes the framing: the permit starts as valid and the government must overcome that presumption, rather than the developer having to defend the permit’s validity.

Vested Rights (State Law Models)

Multiple states have enacted vested rights statutes that protect developers who have substantially relied on permits. The strongest models:

North CarolinaMost comprehensive statutory framework. “A vested right, once established, precludes any action by a local government that would change, alter, impair, prevent, diminish, or otherwise delay the development.”N.C.G.S. §160D-108
ColoradoVesting upon approval of a site-specific development plan. Default 3-year vesting period; can be extended based on project size and market conditions.C.R.S. §24-68-103

Translation: Federal Vested Rights for Energy Permits

A federal vested rights provision could adopt the Washington model (earliest vesting trigger) combined with the North Carolina model (broadest protection):

  • Federal energy permits vest upon issuance (or upon complete application filing, following Washington)
  • Vesting lasts the life of the project or a defined term (e.g., 30 years)
  • Once vested, the government cannot change, alter, impair, prevent, diminish, or delay the project through regulatory action
  • The government bears the burden of proving by clear and convincing evidence that revocation is necessary under one of the narrow exceptions
  • Explicitly preempts subsequent executive orders, agency memoranda, or secretarial orders that would impair vested permits

This is the strongest possible legal protection because it converts a permit from a revocable license into a vested property right that triggers Fifth Amendment takings protections if the government interferes.

Government Must Justify Denials (California Housing Model)

California’s Housing Accountability Act (Gov. Code §65589.5, as amended by SB 167) is the most aggressive burden-shifting statute in U.S. land use law: if a local government denies or reduces a housing project, “the burden of proof shall be on the local legislative body to show that its decision is consistent with the findings… supported by a preponderance of the evidence in the record.”

Translation: Burden on Agency to Justify Permit Denial

Rather than requiring the developer to prove the denial was arbitrary (the current APA standard), the statute could require the agency to prove its denial is justified by a preponderance of evidence in the record. This shifts the courtroom dynamic entirely: the agency is the one that has to put on a case, not the developer.

Permit Shield (Clean Air Act Model)

42 U.S.C. §7661c(f) provides that compliance with a Title V permit “shall be deemed compliance” with the statute itself. This “permit shield” means the permittee can’t be prosecuted for violating the Clean Air Act if they’re complying with their permit terms — even if the permit arguably missed something.

Translation: Energy Permit Shield

A permitting reform could provide: “A project operating in compliance with the terms and conditions of a permit issued under this chapter shall be deemed to be in compliance with all applicable federal environmental requirements covered by the permit. No federal agency may bring an enforcement action against such a project for matters within the scope of the permit while the project remains in compliance.”

2. Remove the Permit Requirement Entirely for Defined Categories

The fastest permit is no permit. Several legal frameworks already allow projects to proceed without individualized agency review if they meet predefined standards.

Permit by Rule (EPA General Permits Model)

Under Section 404 of the Clean Water Act, the Army Corps of Engineers issues Nationwide Permits (NWPs) that pre-authorize entire categories of activity. NWP 51 (Land-Based Renewable Energy) authorizes solar, wind, biomass, and geothermal projects affecting up to 0.5 acres of wetlands. If impacts are under 0.10 acres, no prior notification to the Corps is required at all — the developer simply complies with the general conditions and builds.

Texas TCEQ’s Permit by Rule system (Chapter 106) is even more aggressive: many air quality PBRs are entirely self-implementing with no registration required.

Translation: Energy Permit by Rule

Congress could direct agencies to establish “General Energy Permits” for common project types:

  • Solar on previously disturbed land: If the site was previously developed, mined, farmed, or otherwise disturbed, and the project meets defined setback, stormwater, and species screening criteria — no individual NEPA review required. File a notice and build.
  • Battery storage co-located with existing generation: If sited within the fence line of an existing power plant, no new NEPA review. The facility was already reviewed.
  • Transmission upgrades within existing rights-of-way: Replacing or upgrading existing transmission lines without expanding the footprint. Pre-authorized by general permit.
  • Geothermal on existing oil/gas pads: If the wellpad was already approved and reviewed, geothermal conversion requires only a notification.

Expanded Categorical Exclusions

The Fiscal Responsibility Act of 2023 created Section 109 cross-agency CE adoption, allowing any federal agency to adopt another agency’s categorical exclusions. DOE finalized three new energy CEs in 2024 (energy storage, powerline upgrades, solar PV on disturbed land). But adoption has been slow. Congress could mandate cross-agency adoption and define new statutory CEs for:

  • All energy projects under 20 MW on previously disturbed or brownfield land
  • All transmission reconductoring and voltage upgrades on existing structures
  • All distributed solar installations (rooftop and parking canopy)
  • Geothermal exploration drilling on existing leases
  • Battery storage under 100 MW at sites with existing environmental review

3. If the Government Doesn’t Act, the Permit Is Approved

The most powerful enforcement mechanism isn’t a penalty — it’s automatic approval. Several legal frameworks already use this.

The FCC Shot Clock: Deemed Granted

Under 47 CFR §1.6100, if a local government fails to act on a wireless facility modification within 60 days, the request is automatically deemed granted. The applicant simply sends written notice that the clock expired. No court action needed. No penalties. The permit just… exists.

The FCC is now proposing to extend deemed-granted to all wireless siting applications, including new towers under the 150-day clock.

Translation: Deemed-Approved Energy Permits

The strongest version of this for energy permitting:

  • Routine authorizations: If the agency fails to act within 90 days, the authorization is deemed approved. The applicant sends written notice; the approval takes effect automatically.
  • Complex authorizations: If the agency fails to act within the statutory deadline (1 year for EA, 2 years for EIS), and the applicant has complied with all information requests, the authorization is deemed approved subject to standard conditions.
  • Consulting agency concurrence: Following the CZMA model (16 U.S.C. §1456; 15 CFR Part 930), if a consulting agency (e.g., Fish and Wildlife Service) fails to respond within 6 months, their concurrence is conclusively presumed.

This is more aggressive than either the FREEDOM Act (which triggers contractor substitution and penalties) or the CERTAIN Act (which triggers administrative review). Automatic approval creates an existential incentive for agencies to act on time.

The Legitimate Objection — And Its Answer

Objection: Deemed approval for complex environmental reviews could approve projects with genuinely significant environmental impacts that haven’t been analyzed.

Answer: The deemed-approval trigger should be structured so that it applies only when:

  1. The applicant has submitted a complete application (agency-certified or backstopped by 6-month auto-completion)
  2. The applicant has responded to all agency information requests within the required timelines
  3. The delay is attributable to agency inaction, not incomplete information
  4. Standard environmental conditions automatically attach to any deemed-approved permit (stormwater management, species monitoring, etc.)

California’s Permit Streamlining Act (Gov. Code §65956(b)) has operated this way since 1977 without catastrophic environmental outcomes. The ESA’s Incidental Take Permit process explicitly provides that a project may proceed if the agency fails to act within the statutory consultation timeline.

4. The Enforcement Toolkit: Mechanisms from Other Domains

Beyond the FREEDOM Act’s penalties and the CERTAIN Act’s administrative review, there are far more powerful enforcement mechanisms available from other areas of law.

The FOIA Model: Constructive Exhaustion + Fee Shifting

Under FOIA (5 U.S.C. §552), when an agency misses its 20-day response deadline, the requester is “deemed to have exhausted” administrative remedies and may proceed directly to federal court — bypassing the usual requirement to file an administrative appeal first. Courts review de novo, the burden is on the agency to justify its position, and prevailing requesters recover attorney fees.

A “FOIA for Permits” Enforcement Structure

  1. Establish statutory deadlines for permitting decisions
  2. If the agency misses the deadline, the applicant is deemed to have exhausted administrative remedies and may file directly in federal court — no administrative appeal needed
  3. The court reviews de novo with the burden on the agency to justify the delay
  4. Prevailing applicants recover attorney fees from the agency
  5. This is self-funding: attorneys have an incentive to take cases because fees are recoverable, creating a private enforcement ecosystem

Citizen Suit Provisions

The Clean Air Act (§7604), Clean Water Act (§1365), and other environmental statutes already allow private citizens to sue agencies for failing to perform non-discretionary duties, with fee-shifting to prevailing plaintiffs. These provisions are routinely used to force EPA rulemaking on missed statutory deadlines. The same mechanism could be extended to permitting deadlines.

Qui Tam / False Claims Act Model

Under the False Claims Act (31 U.S.C. §§3729-3733), private citizens can sue on behalf of the government and receive 15-30% of any recovery. FCA recoveries reached $7 billion in FY2025. A “Permitting Claims Act” could allow developers to enforce deadlines with a bounty structure, though this faces constitutional uncertainty after Zafirov v. Florida Medical Associates (M.D. Fla. 2024).

Mandatory Inspector General Triggers

Current IG authority is mostly discretionary. Congress could create mandatory automatic IG investigations triggered by objectively measurable failures:

  • Agency misses deadlines on >20% of permit applications in a fiscal year → mandatory IG audit
  • Cumulative penalties on any single project exceed $500K → mandatory IG investigation of whether the delay was politically directed
  • Agency petitions for voluntary remand of a permit → IG review of whether the petition is substantively justified or politically motivated

Budget Redirection (Modified Sequestration)

When agencies miss permitting targets:

  • Year 1: 5% of the agency’s permitting-related budget is redirected to a Permitting Assistance Fund that hires third-party reviewers
  • Year 2 consecutive miss: 10% redirect + IG audit
  • Year 3 consecutive miss: 15% redirect + mandatory consent decree with federal judicial oversight

The key difference from pure budget cuts: funds are redirected to accelerate permitting, not simply removed. The agency loses control of the money but the work still gets done.

Government Performance Bond

When an agency revokes a previously issued permit, it could be required to post a bond (or set aside escrow funds) equal to the developer’s demonstrated reliance costs. If a court later finds the revocation unlawful, the bond is forfeited to the developer. This creates an immediate financial cost to revocation that concentrates the agency’s mind before acting.

5. Programmatic and Systemic Approaches

Mandatory Programmatic EIS

Instead of reviewing every solar farm individually, the government conducts one programmatic environmental review covering an entire category, region, or technology. Individual projects then “tier” off the programmatic review with a much shorter, narrower analysis.

Best existing example: BLM’s 2024 Solar Programmatic EIS covered 162 million acres across 11 western states. Individual projects in covered areas get fast-tracked because the landscape-level analysis is already done.

Congress Could Mandate Programmatic Reviews For:

  • Onshore solar on BLM, Forest Service, and previously disturbed private land (by region)
  • Onshore wind by state/regional wind resource area
  • Offshore wind by lease area (BOEM already does this for the NY Bight and California)
  • Transmission corridors (building on Section 368 energy corridors)
  • Geothermal by Known Geothermal Resource Area (KGRA)
  • Battery storage co-located with existing generation or within designated zones

Projects within programmatically-reviewed areas that fall within the analyzed parameters would qualify for categorical exclusion from further NEPA review — or at most, a short Environmental Assessment rather than a full EIS.

Pre-Permitted Zones (EU Renewables Acceleration Areas Model)

The EU’s RED III directive (2023/2413) requires every member state to designate “renewables acceleration areas” by February 2026. Within these zones:

  • Individual Environmental Impact Assessments are replaced by simplified screening
  • Member states may entirely exempt wind and solar from project-level environmental assessment
  • Permitting timelines are non-pausable: 12 months in acceleration areas, 3 months for solar installations, 1 month for small solar
  • Renewable energy is declared an “overriding public interest” — it presumptively outweighs competing interests, even in protected Natura 2000 areas

Translation: U.S. Energy Acceleration Zones

The federal government could designate “Energy Acceleration Zones” on federal lands and in federal waters where:

  1. A programmatic environmental review has been completed
  2. Projects meeting specified parameters receive automatic approval within 90 days (solar) or 12 months (wind/transmission)
  3. The environmental review burden is on the zone designation, not on individual projects
  4. This builds on existing BLM Solar Energy Zones and Section 368 energy corridors, but adds the deemed-approved mechanism

7. User-Fee Funding (The PDUFA Model)

The Niskanen Center and Brookings both identify inadequate agency resources as the binding constraint on permitting speed. The most successful model for solving this is the FDA’s Prescription Drug User Fee Act (PDUFA):

  • User fees now generate ~$900 million/year, covering ~80% of drug review costs
  • Before PDUFA, drug reviews took 30+ months; after, standard reviews take ~10 months
  • Fees are tied to negotiated performance timelines (called “PDUFA goals”), creating a direct link between funding and speed
  • The NRC (Nuclear Regulatory Commission) recovers ~100% of its budget through fees

Energy Permitting User Fee Act

Modeled on PDUFA:

  • Project sponsors pay a user fee scaled to project size (e.g., $X per MW of capacity) when submitting permit applications
  • Fees fund dedicated permitting staff at BLM, BOEM, FWS, EPA, Army Corps, and other permitting agencies
  • In exchange, agencies commit to negotiated performance timelines (reviewed every 5 years, like PDUFA reauthorization)
  • If agencies consistently miss performance goals, the fee structure is renegotiated and a portion is redirected to third-party reviewers
  • This creates a self-funding, self-enforcing system: developers pay for speed, agencies are funded to deliver it, and the performance metrics create accountability

The Key Insight: Layered Defenses

No single mechanism is sufficient. An administration determined to block projects will find ways around any one of these. The strength comes from layering:

  1. Layer 1 — Eliminate the permit requirement where possible (permit by rule, categorical exclusions, programmatic review). If no permit is needed, there’s nothing to revoke.
  2. Layer 2 — Make permits property rights. Vesting + presumption of validity + permit shield. The government must overcome a high bar to interfere.
  3. Layer 3 — Non-tollable deadlines with deemed approval. If the government doesn’t act, the permit approves itself. No discretion to stall.
  4. Layer 4 — Financial consequences for obstruction. Government bonds on revocation, budget redirection for missed targets, insurance fund for developer losses.
  5. Layer 5 — Self-executing oversight. Mandatory IG investigations, congressional notification, public dashboard. The administration can’t obstruct quietly.
  6. Layer 6 — Private enforcement ecosystem. Citizen suits with fee-shifting, constructive exhaustion, de novo review. Developers don’t depend on DOJ to enforce their rights.

Each layer makes the others harder to circumvent. An administration that tries to block a project in a pre-permitted zone (Layer 1) must overcome the vested right (Layer 2), explain why it missed the deadline (Layer 3), post a bond (Layer 4), face an IG investigation (Layer 5), and defend itself in court against a fee-shifted private action (Layer 6). That is a formidable obstacle course for obstruction — and an almost frictionless runway for legitimate projects.

9. Self-Executing Enforcement: Laws That Enforce Themselves

The single most important lesson from international experience is that enforcement mechanisms that require discretionary action by the executive branch will fail when the executive branch is the source of the problem. The innovation that separates Germany’s recent success from decades of permitting reform theater is the concept of self-executing enforcement — laws that automatically change the legal default when a target is missed, without requiring any government official to pull a trigger.

The German WindBG: “Comply or Lose Control”

Germany’s Wind-an-Land-Gesetz (WaLG) creates the most sophisticated self-executing enforcement architecture in any energy law worldwide. It works through an ingenious inversion:

If the state…Then…
Meets its area target (1.4% by 2027, 2.0% by 2032)The state retains full planning control. Wind turbines outside designated areas lose their privileged status under §35 BauGB. The state can steer development to the areas it prefers.
Fails to meet its area targetWind turbines automatically regain universal privileged status across all rural areas. All planning reservations, concentration zone exclusions, distance rules, and regional planning restrictions that previously blocked wind development become legally ineffective. The state loses all ability to restrict where wind turbines are built.

The mechanism is truly automatic. No federal authority needs to “declare” non-compliance. The legal consequence is embedded in the statute itself, triggered by the passage of a deadline without sufficient area designation. Article 31 of the Basic Law (“Federal law breaks state law”) ensures that conflicting state planning measures are overridden by operation of law.

Bavaria: The Proof That It Works

Bavaria’s “10H rule” (2014) required wind turbines to be at least 10 times their height from residential buildings — effectively banning new onshore wind in Germany’s largest state. Under the WindBG, Bavaria faced a binary choice: relax the 10H rule voluntarily and maintain some planning control, or lose all planning authority when the federal targets kicked in.

Bavaria chose to comply. In November 2022, the CSU government — which had originally enacted the 10H rule — relaxed it to 1,000 meters in select areas. The result: 743 new turbine applications in the first six months of 2025 alone, more than the entire years 2022–2024 combined. 96 permits were issued by end of June 2025, more than in the entire previous year. The 10H rule didn’t need to be litigated or negotiated — the self-executing mechanism made compliance the rational choice.

The Perverse Incentive Problem

German legal scholars have identified a design flaw worth noting: for some states, intentional non-compliance may be politically rational. A state government facing strong local opposition to wind energy might prefer to “fail” at meeting targets, triggering the automatic override — and then blame the federal government for the resulting wind development. The state avoids the political cost of designating areas while wind energy still expands. Any U.S. translation would need to account for this accountability diffusion problem.

Other Self-Executing Mechanisms in Law

Germany’s WindBG is not an isolated experiment. Self-executing enforcement has precedents across multiple domains:

MechanismDomainHow It Works
Clean Air Act §181(b)(2) U.S. air quality When an area fails to attain air quality standards by its deadline, it is reclassified by operation of law to the next higher category (e.g., “Marginal” → “Moderate”), automatically imposing more stringent requirements, offset ratios, and sanctions. The Design Pattern

Every successful self-executing mechanism shares the same structure:

  1. A quantitative, verifiable trigger — area percentage, housing ratio, air quality reading, days elapsed. No subjective judgment needed.
  2. An automatic legal consequence — the default rule changes without requiring any official to take action. The statute itself defines what happens.
  3. The consequence is worse than compliance — the state or locality would prefer to meet the target and maintain control rather than face the automatic override.
  4. No veto point — no official, at any level of government, can prevent the consequence from taking effect once the trigger is met.

10. Anti-Backsliding: How to Make Reform Stick

Self-executing enforcement addresses one problem: how to force compliance when government entities are recalcitrant. But there’s a harder problem: how to prevent a future administration from dismantling the reform itself. This is the anti-backsliding question, and the EU’s binding directive framework is the most developed answer anywhere in the world.

The EU’s Multi-Layered Lock-In

When the EU adopts a directive like RED III, it creates six independent enforcement vectors that a member state government must overcome simultaneously to backslide:

LayerMechanismWhat Happens
1Infringement proceedings (Art. 258 TFEU)The European Commission — an independent body insulated from national politics — opens formal proceedings. 95% of cases resolve before reaching court. Letters of formal notice were sent to 26 of 27 member states in September 2024 over RED III permitting.
2Financial penalties (Art. 260 TFEU)The Lisbon Treaty (2009) allows the Commission to request penalties in the first referral for non-transposition. Actual penalties: Germany was fined €34 million for failing to transpose the Whistleblower Directive. Poland faced €500,000/day for air quality violations. Sweden became the first RED III case referred to the CJEU (October 2025) with financial penalties requested.

A developer in a non-compliant member state could potentially cite RED III’s 24-month timeline directly. The overriding public interest presumption (Art. 16f) is the strongest candidate for direct effect because it is clear, precise, and unconditional.
4Francovich state liabilityMember states are liable in damages to individuals who suffer loss from failure to transpose a directive. A developer who can show quantifiable losses from permitting delays exceeding RED III timelines could claim damages against the non-transposing state.
5Statutory lock-inTransposing legislation is enacted by parliament, not by executive order. Repealing it requires a legislative majority — and even then, the EU obligation persists independently. Repeal immediately reopens infringement proceedings, Francovich liability, and direct effect.
6Non-regression principleEmerging in EU environmental law: member states may not reduce existing levels of environmental protection. Environmental non-regression has been argued to be a general principle of EU law, which would give it near-constitutional status.

The core problem: in the U.S., if a future president decides to obstruct permitting, the tools to stop them are weak. Executive orders can be reversed overnight. Agency guidance can be withdrawn instantly. Even statutory deadlines can be evaded through underfunding, understaffing, or creative interpretation. The EU model shows that durable reform requires multiple overlapping enforcement vectors that are independent of the political branch being constrained.

The Track Record: Does EU Enforcement Actually Work?

The honest answer: formal transposition, yes; substantive compliance, partially.

  • EU enforcement is excellent at forcing laws onto the books — 95% of infringement cases resolve before reaching court
  • EU enforcement is considerably weaker at changing behavior on the ground. Even where permitting timelines are formally transposed, actual processing times have not universally accelerated. As of July 2025, the average member state had transposed just under 50% of RED III permitting measures — more than a year past the July 2024 deadline
  • Financial penalties have genuine deterrent value: the €34M German fine and €500K/day Polish penalty demonstrate real cost
  • The average infringement case takes ~3.8 years to resolve, and environmental cases can take 6–10 years
  • The GDPR comparison is instructive: enforcement is fastest and most effective where decentralized enforcement bodies (like data protection authorities) can impose fines directly, without going through a central court

The implication for U.S. design: relying on a single enforcement pathway (courts, or an agency, or Congress) is insufficient. The strongest architecture uses multiple independent enforcement vectors — any one of which can independently force compliance.

11. A “Clean Permitting Act”: Translating These Models to U.S. Law

The question is not whether self-executing enforcement and anti-backsliding mechanisms can work. They already work — in German energy law, in the Clean Air Act, in California housing law, in the EU directive framework. The question is how to translate them into a U.S. permitting statute that survives constitutional scrutiny.

Constitutional Constraints

Three Doctrines to Navigate

  • Anti-commandeering (10th Amendment): Congress cannot compel states to enact or enforce a federal regulatory program (New York v. United States, 1992; Printz, 1997; Murphy v. NCAA, 2018). But a WindBG-style mechanism may survive because it does not require states to do anything. Like the Clean Air Act’s SIP system, it defines federal consequences of state inaction rather than compelling state action. Germany’s mechanism survived constitutional scrutiny for exactly this reason — the Federal Constitutional Court held in the Thuringia Forest Decision (2022) that the WaLG operates through established federal legislative competence, not commandeering.
  • Spending Clause coercion (NFIB v. Sebelius): Congress can condition federal funds on state cooperation, but the amount at stake cannot be so large as to be “coercive.” The coercion threshold is somewhere between 5% (Dole, upheld) and Medicaid-level stakes (~10% of state budget, struck down). A permitting reform act could condition federal energy grants on state compliance without hitting this threshold.
  • Nondelegation: Congress may create automatic triggers tied to objective, factual determinations. A WindBG-style mechanism that automatically changes permitting rules when a state misses a quantitative target would likely survive because Congress specifies both the trigger condition and the consequence.

The SIP/FIP Model: The Strongest Constitutional Pathway

Cooperative Federalism with Federal Fallback

The Clean Air Act’s State Implementation Plan (SIP) / Federal Implementation Plan (FIP) framework is the most structurally analogous U.S. mechanism to EU directive enforcement — and it has been upheld by the Supreme Court for 50 years. Adapted to energy permitting, it would work as follows:

  1. Congress sets federal permitting standards: Maximum permitting timelines for categories of energy projects (e.g., 12 months for solar in pre-screened areas, 24 months for wind, 6 months for battery storage). One-stop-shop coordination requirements. Overriding public interest presumption for clean energy projects.
  2. States submit State Permitting Plans (SPPs): States have 18 months to submit plans demonstrating how they will achieve these timelines, including one-stop-shop designations, staffing plans, and procedural streamlining. This preserves state primacy and flexibility.
  3. Federal review for adequacy: DOE or FERC reviews SPPs using objective criteria. If adequate, the state retains full permitting authority.
  4. Federal Permitting Plan (FPP) fallback: If a state fails to submit an adequate SPP — or submits one but then fails to meet its own timelines — the federal agency steps in with a Federal Permitting Plan. Federal permitting authority supersedes state authority for covered projects. The federal government actually issues the permits itself.
  5. Self-executing sanctions: Modeled on the CAA, sanctions apply automatically when a state fails: (a) offset requirements tighten, (b) a portion of federal energy grants is withheld, (c) the federal permitting pathway becomes available to developers as an alternative.
  6. Citizen suit provisions: Modeled on CAA §304. Private parties can sue to enforce the statute directly in federal court, including against federal agencies that fail to impose FPPs or process permits within statutory timelines. Attorney’s fees are shifted to the prevailing plaintiff.

The Critical Advantage Over the EU Model

The U.S. cooperative federalism framework has one crucial advantage over the EU directive model: the federal government can actually do the permitting itself. The EU Commission cannot process permits in a non-compliant member state — it can only fine the member state until it complies. Under the SIP/FIP model, if a state refuses to permit energy projects within statutory timelines, the federal government bypasses the state entirely and issues permits directly. This eliminates the compliance gap that plagues EU enforcement.

This is not hypothetical. EPA already administers Federal Implementation Plans in states that fail to submit adequate air quality plans. FERC already has backstop siting authority for interstate electric transmission under the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (2021). The legal infrastructure exists.

Making It Stick: The Anti-Backsliding Package

To prevent a future administration from dismantling the framework, Congress would embed multiple independent enforcement vectors:

  1. Mandatory duties (“the Secretary shall…”) rather than discretionary authority (“the Secretary may…”). This creates the basis for mandamus actions if a future administration attempts to abandon enforcement. Courts can order officials to perform nondiscretionary duties.
  2. Citizen suit provisions with fee-shifting, modeled on CAA §304. Any person can sue to enforce the statute — the developer, an environmental group, a state attorney general, a competitor. Enforcement does not depend on DOJ.
  3. Self-executing triggers (WindBG model). If the federal agency fails to impose a Federal Permitting Plan within the statutory deadline, permits in non-compliant states are deemed approved after the statutory timeline expires. No official needs to act.
  4. Independent oversight. An inspector general, an independent permitting council (like FPISC), or a GAO reporting mandate creates monitoring that cannot be shut down by the executive branch alone.
  5. Statutory lock-in. The framework is enacted as a statute, not an executive order. Repealing it requires a legislative majority in both chambers plus a presidential signature (or a veto override). This is the most basic but most important protection — and it’s exactly what the Burgum memo couldn’t have survived if the FREEDOM Act or CERTAIN Act had already been law.

The Complete Architecture: 6 Layers + Self-Executing + Anti-Backsliding

Integrating self-executing enforcement and anti-backsliding provisions with the 6 Layered Defenses produces the most obstruction-resistant permitting framework possible under the U.S. Constitution:

LayerMechanismEnforcement Type
1Eliminate the permit requirement (CEs, permit by right, pre-permitted zones)Self-executing: if project meets criteria, no permit needed. Nothing to obstruct.
2Make permits property rights (vesting, presumption, permit shield)Judicially enforceable: developer sues under due process if government interferes.
3Non-tollable deadlines with deemed approvalSelf-executing: deadline passes = approval granted by operation of law.
4Financial consequences (insurance fund, bonds, budget redirection)Self-executing: penalties accrue automatically. Bond posted before revocation takes effect.
5Mandatory oversight (IG, congressional notification, public dashboard)Institutional: independent bodies that cannot be shut down by the executive alone.
6Private enforcement (citizen suits, fee-shifting, constructive exhaustion)Decentralized: any affected party can enforce. Does not depend on DOJ or any single agency.

An administration attempting to block a project through this framework would need to simultaneously: prevent the project from qualifying for categorical exclusion (Layer 1), overcome a vested property right (Layer 2), explain why a self-executing deadline didn’t apply (Layer 3), post a financial bond (Layer 4), survive an IG investigation (Layer 5), defend against a fee-shifted private lawsuit (Layer 6), prevent the automatic opening of a federal permitting pathway (Layer 7), and resist a mandamus action compelling the federal fallback (Layer 8). That is not a political debate. That is an obstacle course that makes obstruction more expensive than compliance — which is exactly the point.

XI. How Other Countries Do This

The United States is not the only country struggling with energy permitting. But it is one of the few wealthy democracies that has failed to enact binding statutory timelines, one-stop-shop coordination, or deemed-approval mechanisms for energy projects. The international landscape offers a rich library of solutions — some successful, some cautionary — that can directly inform the next generation of American permitting reform.

What follows is a comprehensive survey of permitting regimes across four continents, organized to extract the specific mechanisms, timelines, and legal innovations that translate most directly to the FREEDOM Act / CERTAIN Act debate.

The EU’s Renewable Energy Directive III: The World’s Most Comprehensive Permitting Framework

The EU’s Renewable Energy Directive III (Directive 2023/2413, or “RED III”), adopted in October 2023, represents the most ambitious legislative attempt anywhere in the world to systematically accelerate energy permitting. It is binding on all 27 EU member states, with transposition deadlines running through May 2025 and full implementation of acceleration areas by February 2026.

RED III’s Five Pillars

  1. Binding maximum timelines for all renewable energy permits, varying by project type and location
  2. Renewable Acceleration Areas (RAAs) — pre-designated zones where environmental review is replaced by a simplified screening process
  3. Overriding Public Interest (OPI) — a statutory presumption that renewables serve the public interest, shifting the burden in legal challenges
  4. Tacit approval for administrative sub-steps and small-scale projects when deadlines are missed (with three exceptions: projects requiring EIA, final decisions, and member states where tacit approval doesn’t exist in the legal system)
  5. One-stop-shop requirement for all member states — a single coordinating authority for each project

The Overriding Public Interest Presumption (Article 16f)

Until climate neutrality is achieved, the planning, construction, and operation of renewable energy power plants — including their grid connections, storage, and associated infrastructure — are presumed to be of overriding public interest and to serve public health and safety. This is not a policy aspiration; it is a binding legal standard that shifts the burden in every court and administrative proceeding. A challenger must overcome the presumption, rather than the developer proving their project serves the public.

This is the single most aggressive burden-shift in any permitting regime in the world, and it maps directly to the “burden-shifting” concepts discussed in Section X.

Acceleration Areas: Individual EIA Replaced by Screening

Under Article 15c, member states must designate Renewable Acceleration Areas (RAAs) by February 21, 2026. Within RAAs, individual Environmental Impact Assessments under the EIA Directive are replaced by a simplified two-step screening process. The area itself undergoes a Strategic Environmental Assessment during designation, and individual projects within it are screened for specific mitigation measures rather than subjected to full-scale environmental review. This is the European equivalent of expanded categorical exclusions — but with a geographic basis rather than a project-type basis.

TEN-E: Cross-Border Infrastructure

For major cross-border energy infrastructure (Projects of Common Interest), the EU’s TEN-E Regulation (Regulation 347/2013, revised 2022) establishes a separate permitting regime: a mandatory one-stop-shop competent authority per member state, a total permit-granting timeline of 3.5 years (24-month pre-application + 18-month statutory decision period), and a “manual of procedures” published by each member state. The TEN-E model is the closest European analogue to the U.S. FAST-41 framework.

Germany: Land Allocation Targets and Overriding Public Interest

Germany has historically been one of the slowest permitters in Europe, with onshore wind projects averaging 4–7 years from application to commissioning. Beginning in 2022, the Scholz government passed a suite of emergency laws that represent the most dramatic permitting acceleration attempted by any single country.

Wind-an-Land-Gesetz (WaLG) — The Onshore Wind Act

  • 2% land target: All 13 territorial states (Flächenländer) must designate 1.4% of their area for wind energy by 2027 and 2.0% by 2032. City-states (Berlin, Hamburg, Bremen) must designate 0.5%.
  • Automatic consequence for non-compliance: If a state fails to meet its target, wind turbines automatically gain “privileged status” under §35 of the Building Code (BauGB), overriding local distance rules and zoning restrictions. This is a rare example of a self-executing enforcement mechanism — the government doesn’t need to sue; the law simply changes the default.
  • Federal guidance, state execution: States do their own spatial planning but must follow uniform federal methodology. This preserves federalism while preventing NIMBYism from blocking national goals.

Overriding Public Interest Declaration

Germany declared that renewable energy installations, including wind, solar, and associated grid infrastructure, serve the “overriding public interest” and public safety. This declaration, codified in the Renewable Energy Act (EEG 2023), has the same legal effect as the EU’s Article 16f: in any balancing test (species protection, landscape protection, water law), renewables start with a legal thumb on the scale. Courts must give particular weight to the energy transition when evaluating project impacts.

BImSchG Reform: Statutory Decision Deadlines

Germany also reformed its primary environmental permitting statute (the Federal Immission Control Act, BImSchG) in 2024 to impose binding decision deadlines:

  • Simplified procedure (wind farms <20 turbines): 3 months from receipt of complete application, extendable once by 3 months. No public participation required.
  • Formal procedure (larger installations): 7 months, extendable once by 3 months. Includes online public notice and comment period.
  • Repowering: Replacement of existing turbines with more efficient ones qualifies for simplified procedures regardless of capacity.

Compare these timelines to the U.S., where NEPA review alone averages 4.5 years for an EIS. Germany’s statutory maximum for the entire permitting decision — including environmental review — is shorter than the average U.S. scoping period.

Impact: Germany approved 10 GW of new onshore wind annually from 2025 onward, up from ~2 GW/year in 2019–2021. The 2% land target and automatic privileged status have been credited as the primary drivers. The EEG 2023 targets: 115 GW onshore wind and 215 GW solar PV by 2030, with 80% of gross electricity from renewables.

Denmark: The Gold Standard One-Stop-Shop

Denmark consistently ranks as the fastest permitting jurisdiction in Europe, and its model is the most-cited international example in U.S. permitting reform debates.

Key Features

  • Danish Energy Agency (DEA) as one-stop-shop: A single agency coordinates all permits for energy projects. The developer submits one application; the DEA manages inter-agency coordination internally. The developer never needs to navigate between agencies.
  • Guaranteed timelines: Onshore wind and solar permits are processed within 24 months. Offshore wind (including tender process) within 36 months. Small-scale solar and repowering within 6–12 months.
  • Community ownership requirements: Developers must offer at least 20% ownership to local communities, which reduces local opposition and accelerates community consultation. Denmark has found that financial participation is more effective than public hearings at building support.
  • Pre-screened areas: The Danish government pre-designates areas suitable for wind and solar development, conducting environmental screening at the area level rather than the project level. Projects within these areas face simplified review.
  • Ministerial override for national projects: For projects of national significance, the Minister of Climate can override local objections — though this power is rarely exercised because the community ownership model generally prevents impasses.
Why Denmark succeeds: It combines binding timelines + one-stop-shop + community buy-in + pre-screened areas. No single mechanism explains Danish speed; it’s the layered combination. This maps directly to the “6 Layered Defenses” model in Section X.

France: Acceleration Zones and the Préfectoral Referent

France enacted Law No. 2023-175 (March 10, 2023), its own renewable energy acceleration law, responding to a deployment rate that had fallen far behind its EU commitments.

Key Mechanisms

  • Municipal acceleration zones: Municipalities must define zones where renewable energy installations are encouraged. Unlike Germany’s top-down land allocation targets, France’s approach is bottom-up: communes identify their own best sites based on local potential and existing capacity. The trade-off is that this can be slower than Germany’s approach, but it preserves stronger local democratic buy-in.
  • Préfectoral Referent: Each region gets a single “referent préfectoral” — a state representative who serves as the single entry point for permit applications. This is France’s version of a one-stop-shop, operating at the regional level.
  • Regional Energy Committees: New bodies that coordinate between municipalities, regional authorities, and the national government on renewable energy targets.
  • Mandatory solar on commercial buildings: Parking lots exceeding 1,500 m² must install solar canopies (phased: >10,000 m² by July 2023; >1,500 m² by July 2026). New commercial and public buildings over 500 m² must include solar or green roofing. These mandates effectively create permit-by-right pathways for entire project categories.
  • RIIPM (Overriding Public Interest): Qualifying renewable energy projects receive the legal status of raison impérative d’intérêt public majeur — imperative reason of overriding public interest. This is France’s transposition of the RED III Article 16f concept, with direct legal consequences for derogations under the Habitats Directive and species protection law.

Netherlands: 26 Laws Consolidated into One

The Netherlands enacted the Omgevingswet (Environment and Planning Act), which took effect January 1, 2024, consolidating 26 separate environmental and planning laws into a single statute with a unified permit system.

Omgevingswet: The “All-in-One” Permit

  • One application, one authority, one decision: The Wabo (General Environmental Law Act, 2010), now folded into the Omgevingswet, consolidated approximately 25 formerly separate permit, license, and exemption systems into a single “all-in-one” environmental permit covering up to 19 activity categories. Before 2010, a developer might need separate permits for building (Housing Act), environmental management, spatial planning derogations, monuments, felling, and more — each from different authorities with different procedures. The Wabo ended that. The Omgevingswet went further, merging 26 laws, 60 orders in council, and 75 ministerial regulations into one statute.
  • 8-week standard timeline: For regular procedures, applications are processed within 8 weeks. Extended procedures for complex projects have a 6-month timeline. This compares to the U.S. average of 4.5 years for an EIS under NEPA.
  • Digital-first: All applications, consultations, and decisions go through a single digital platform (the “Omgevingsloket”), creating transparency and reducing coordination delays.
The consolidation lesson: The Netherlands didn’t eliminate environmental review — it eliminated the bureaucratic duplication. The same environmental questions get asked, but they’re asked once, by one authority, with one timeline. This is the most direct analogue to what the CERTAIN Act’s process coordination provisions attempt, but far more comprehensive.

Spain: Stepped Permitting with Size Thresholds

Spain uses a tiered system where permitting requirements escalate with project size. Projects below 50 MW are permitted by Autonomous Communities (regional governments); above 50 MW by the national Directorate General. Royal Decree-Law 6/2022 introduced simplified environmental assessment for solar projects not on protected land, and allowed joint processing of prior administrative authorization, construction permits, and declaration of public utility. The target: 3 months for administrative decisions once environmental clearance is obtained. Spain has authorized 25 GW of solar PV capacity under this streamlined framework.

United Kingdom: Nationally Significant Infrastructure Projects

The UK’s Planning Act 2008 created a dedicated permitting regime for large energy projects that separates them from the normal planning system entirely.

The NSIP / Development Consent Order (DCO) Regime

  • Nationally Significant Infrastructure Projects (NSIPs) above capacity thresholds (e.g., >50 MW onshore, >100 MW offshore wind) go through a specialized process administered by the Planning Inspectorate, not local councils.
  • Statutory timeline: The examination phase is capped at 6 months. The decision must be made within 3 months after examination. Total statutory timeline: ~18 months from application acceptance to decision, though pre-application consultation adds 12–18 months.
  • Development Consent Order: A single order grants all necessary permissions — planning permission, compulsory purchase, listed building consent, highway access, environmental permits. This is the UK’s version of a comprehensive one-stop-shop, and it eliminates the sequential permit problem entirely.
  • National Policy Statements (NPSs): The government publishes binding policy statements establishing the need for specific infrastructure types. The Planning Inspectorate must give these “substantial weight.” This means developers don’t need to re-prove that the country needs wind energy in every single application.

Ofgem Energy Regulation Sandbox

The UK’s energy regulator, Ofgem, operates a regulatory sandbox where innovative energy projects can apply for temporary regulatory relief to test new business models. While not a permitting sandbox per se, it establishes the principle that certain innovative projects should face lighter-touch regulation during their development phase. This concept could translate to a “permitting sandbox” for novel clean energy technologies.

The Onshore Wind Exception: A Cautionary Tale

From 2015 to 2023, the UK effectively banned new onshore wind projects in England by removing their eligibility for the NSIP regime and requiring local planning consent plus community support. This demonstrated that even strong permitting frameworks can be undermined by political carve-outs. The ban was lifted in late 2023, but it resulted in nearly a decade of lost deployment. The lesson for U.S. legislators: permitting reform must be technology-neutral and resistant to future political interference — exactly the argument the FREEDOM Act makes.

Nordic Countries: Contrasting Approaches

Norway: The Petroleum Model

Norway’s APA (Awards in Predefined Areas) system for petroleum licensing pre-screens mature exploration areas and runs annual licensing rounds with fixed timelines. Environmental assessment is done at the area level; individual licenses face only project-specific review. Average time from license award to production decision: 2–3 years. Norway has begun extending this model to offshore wind through its 2021 Energy Act amendments, with pre-screened offshore wind areas opening for competitive licensing. The relevance for the U.S.: Norway demonstrates that even in a country with strong environmental protections, area-based pre-screening dramatically accelerates project-level permitting.

Sweden: The Municipal Veto — What Not to Do

Sweden maintains a de facto municipal veto over wind energy projects, allowing any municipality to block a project regardless of national energy goals. The result: Sweden has among the highest wind resources in Europe but a project rejection rate exceeding 70% in many counties. The IEA and European Commission have both cited Sweden as a cautionary example of how local veto power can undermine national energy policy. The lesson: any permitting reform that gives local authorities unqualified blocking power will fail, no matter how good the rest of the framework is.

Finland: Nuclear Licensing as a Model

Finland’s nuclear licensing regime is often cited as a model for structured, high-scrutiny permitting: a parliamentary decision-in-principle followed by a two-phase construction/operating license from STUK (the radiation safety authority). Despite rigorous safety review, the process is predictable and follows a defined sequence with statutory consultation periods. Finland demonstrates that even for the most safety-sensitive projects, structured timelines and clear sequencing can coexist with thorough review.

Australia: EPBC Reform and Bilateral Agreements

Australia’s Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation (EPBC) Act has been undergoing reform since the landmark Samuel Review (2020), which found the Act “not fit for purpose” and recommended a wholesale restructuring.

Key Mechanisms

  • Bilateral agreements: The federal government accredits state/territory assessment processes to avoid duplication. If a state process meets federal standards, the federal government accepts the state’s assessment rather than conducting its own. This is Australia’s version of “one-stop-shop” within a federal system — and the most direct model for how U.S. federal-state permitting duplication could be addressed.
  • National Environmental Standard: A proposed single national standard would replace the current case-by-case ministerial discretion, making outcomes more predictable.
  • Offshore wind regulatory framework: Australia established a bespoke offshore wind regulatory framework (Offshore Electricity Infrastructure Act 2021) with four stages: feasibility license → commercial license → construction → operations. Timelines: feasibility license within 6 months, commercial license within 12 months. The framework was designed from scratch based on lessons from other countries’ offshore wind permitting failures.
  • Community benefit sharing: Following Denmark’s lead, Australia has begun requiring community benefit-sharing arrangements for large renewable energy projects, finding (as Denmark did) that financial participation reduces opposition more effectively than consultation processes.

Asia-Pacific: Scale and Speed

South Korea: The Special Act (2025)

South Korea’s 2025 Special Act on Facilitation of Clean Energy Projects is the most aggressive “deemed permit” regime in the world. When the primary energy project permit is issued, 28 other permits across 11 different laws are deemed to have been simultaneously attained. These include permits under the Road Act, River Act, Farmland Act, Forest Protection Act, Mountainous District Management Act, and others. The developer doesn’t apply separately for any of them. This is the logical endpoint of the “one permit to rule them all” approach — and it goes far beyond what either the FREEDOM Act or the CERTAIN Act contemplates.

India: PARIVESH — Digital Integration

India’s PARIVESH portal (Pro-Active and Responsive facilitation by Interactive and Virtuous Environmental Single-window Hub) integrates environmental, forest, wildlife, and CRZ clearances into a single digital platform with tracked timelines and automatic escalation. While India’s permitting still faces significant delays in practice, the digital infrastructure provides full transparency: applicants and the public can see exactly where each application stands, which authority is holding it, and how long it has been pending. The dashboard model is a direct analogue to what the FREEDOM Act’s permitting performance provisions attempt.

Japan: Dual-Track System

Japan operates a dual-track permitting system for renewable energy: a rapid track for projects below capacity thresholds (typically processed within 3–6 months) and a comprehensive environmental assessment track for larger projects (12–36 months). The EIA process uses a “scoping document” that limits environmental review to issues identified as material at the outset, preventing scope creep. Japan’s offshore wind permitting has been restructured around designated “promotion areas” where environmental surveys are conducted by the government before developers even bid, effectively front-loading the environmental work.

New Zealand: Fast-Track Approvals Act (2024)

New Zealand’s Fast-Track Approvals Act creates a parallel permitting pathway for “nationally or regionally significant” infrastructure projects. Expert panels make recommendations; a joint ministers panel makes final decisions. The Act explicitly overrides normal Resource Management Act procedures and includes a “one-stop-shop” mechanism that bundles resource consents, designations, and other approvals into a single process. Environmental safeguards remain but are assessed at the project level by the expert panel rather than through sequential agency review.

Other Asia-Pacific

Taiwan has created offshore wind “zonal development areas” where environmental impact assessments are conducted by the government before site allocation, allowing projects to begin construction within 18 months of winning a tender. Vietnam has used power development master plans (PDP) to pre-approve locations for renewable energy development, though implementation has been inconsistent. Singapore, while geographically constrained, has implemented permit-by-right for rooftop solar on all commercial buildings, eliminating the permit requirement entirely for the largest category of deployable projects.

Americas: Mixed Results

Canada: The Impact Assessment Act Saga

Canada’s experience is the most directly relevant cautionary tale for U.S. legislators. The Impact Assessment Act (2019) attempted to modernize federal environmental review with binding timelines (300 days for standard assessments, 600 days for comprehensive ones). But in October 2023, the Supreme Court of Canada ruled large portions of the Act unconstitutional, finding the federal government had overreached into provincial jurisdiction. The ruling invalidated the “designated projects” framework and the federal assessment process for most projects. Canada is now rebuilding its framework through Bill C-69 amendments, with a greater emphasis on federal-provincial cooperation rather than federal override. The lesson: in a federal system, permitting reform must respect the constitutional allocation of powers, or courts will invalidate it.

Canada’s Bill C-49 (2024) for offshore wind in the Atlantic provinces (Nova Scotia, Newfoundland) created a co-management model between federal and provincial regulators, with a single regulatory body handling both petroleum and offshore renewables. This avoids the constitutional problems of the IAA by building on the existing Atlantic Accord framework.

Brazil: IBAMA and the Fast-Track Experiment

Brazil’s environmental licensing through IBAMA (Brazilian Institute of Environment) uses a three-phase system: Preliminary License (LP) → Installation License (LI) → Operation License (LO). In 2023, Brazil introduced “Priority Environmental Assessment” for renewable energy projects, moving them to the front of the queue and setting 12-month targets for LP issuance. Brazil has also adopted “simplified licensing” for projects under certain size thresholds and in pre-approved corridors. The challenge: IBAMA is chronically understaffed, so statutory timelines are frequently missed. This illustrates the FREEDOM Act’s insight that deadlines without agency capacity funding are performative.

Chile: Single-Window Environmental Assessment

Chile’s Environmental Assessment Service (Servicio de Evaluación Ambiental, or SEA) operates a genuine single-window system for environmental review. All environmental permits and sectoral authorizations are processed through one agency, and the environmental resolution (RCA) serves as an integrated approval covering all environmental requirements. Projects under certain thresholds (typically <3 MW for solar) use a “Declaration of Environmental Impact” (DIA) rather than a full Environmental Impact Study (EIA), with processing times of 60–120 days. Chile’s system is widely regarded as the best in Latin America.

Mexico

Mexico’s energy permitting has been volatile, with the AMLO administration (2018–2024) effectively blocking new renewable energy permits through regulatory changes and constitutional amendments that favored state-owned CFE. This demonstrates the same vulnerability the U.S. is experiencing: permitting regimes that depend on executive branch cooperation can be weaponized when political priorities change. Mexico is now attempting to rebuild investor confidence through new permitting frameworks under the Sheinbaum administration.

What the International Evidence Tells Us

What Fast-Permitting Countries Have in Common

Across all the successful models — Denmark, Netherlands, EU RED III, South Korea, Chile, Australia — a clear pattern emerges. Fast-permitting countries share all five of the following features. Countries missing any one of them are significantly slower.

  1. One-stop-shop coordination: A single authority coordinates all permits. The developer deals with one entity. Internal inter-agency coordination is the government’s problem, not the developer’s.
  2. Binding statutory timelines: Not aspirational targets — hard deadlines with consequences (deemed approval, automatic permission, budget consequences, or legal remedies).
  3. Area-based pre-screening: Environmental review is front-loaded at the area level rather than repeated for each project. Individual projects in pre-screened areas face simplified review.
  4. Size-based tiering: Small projects face minimal or no permitting. Medium projects face streamlined review. Only very large projects face full assessment. This matches regulatory burden to actual environmental risk.
  5. Political insulation: The permitting framework is resistant to executive branch manipulation. Whether through statutory presumptions, self-executing enforcement, or independent regulatory bodies, fast-permitting countries have found ways to prevent political interference with the permitting process.

What Slow-Permitting Countries Have in Common

  • Local veto power without override mechanisms (Sweden, pre-2023 UK onshore wind, many U.S. jurisdictions)
  • Sequential multi-agency review where each agency operates independently with its own timeline (United States, pre-reform Germany, Brazil)
  • Deadlines without consequences — statutory timelines that agencies routinely miss without penalty (Brazil, pre-reform Australia, many U.S. statutes)
  • Political vulnerability — permitting frameworks that can be subverted by executive action (Mexico, United States, pre-reform Canada)
  • Lack of agency resources dedicated to timely processing (Brazil, United States under sequestration)

The Gap — and the Opportunity

The international evidence makes the scale of the U.S. gap painfully clear. Even the more aggressive of the two bills (the FREEDOM Act) would, if enacted, still leave the United States behind the EU, Denmark, the Netherlands, South Korea, and several other countries on most dimensions. Both bills are necessary steps — but neither bill, standing alone, would bring the U.S. to international best practice.

The mechanisms that could close the gap — area-based pre-screening, overriding public interest presumptions, size-based tiering, digital single-window systems, community benefit-sharing requirements, and self-executing enforcement — are not speculative ideas. They are proven, operational systems deployed at scale in democracies with strong environmental protections and rule of law. The question for Congress is not whether these mechanisms work. It is whether the U.S. is willing to adopt them.

The Airtight Strategy

Closing every door — known and unknown — to executive obstruction of energy projects.

Both the FREEDOM and CERTAIN Acts respond to the correct crisis: an executive branch that has weaponized every lever of federal authority to block disfavored energy projects. But they focus primarily on the obvious agencies — Interior, EPA, Army Corps — while leaving dozens of side doors wide open. A hostile administration doesn’t need to go through the front door. It can use the FAA, DOT, IRS, Commerce Department, CFIUS, Coast Guard, or any of a dozen other authorities to achieve the same result.

This proposal maps every federal authority that can obstruct an energy project, catalogs every method of obstruction deployed in 2025–2026, and designs a 12-provision statutory framework that blocks them all — including attacks nobody has thought of yet.

Topline Argument

Both bills protect the front door. The problem is there are 20 doors.

The Burgum memo, the offshore wind stop-work orders, the eagle law crackdown, the IPaC system shutdown, the IRS safe harbor elimination, the BLM fee hike, the DOT setback rule, the Corps permitting pause — that’s eight different agencies using eight different authorities in twelve months. The FREEDOM and CERTAIN Acts cover maybe three of them well.

The structural issue: “Authorization” in both bills means permits and approvals from the agencies everyone thinks about — Interior, EPA, Corps. But a wind project also needs an FAA Determination of No Hazard (no statutory deadline), a DOD radar compatibility review (no statutory deadline), an NHPA Section 106 consultation (no statutory deadline), and an eagle take permit (which FWS can simply not process). A single missing authorization from a single obscure agency kills the project just as dead as a BLM denial. And the economic kill switches — IRS revoking safe harbors, Commerce slapping 249% tariffs on solar components, BLM imposing a 3.9% gross revenue fee — can destroy a fully permitted project without touching a single permit.

What this proposal does that the current bills don’t — three things:

  1. Universal coverage. Define “authorization” to cover everything — FAA determinations, DOD reviews, Coast Guard permits, IRS certifications, FERC interconnection, and a catch-all for anything we haven’t thought of yet. If a federal action is required for the project to proceed, it’s covered. Period.
  2. Deemed approval. Both bills have consequences for missed deadlines — penalties, contractor substitution, administrative review. Neither has automatic approval. Penalties can be absorbed. Administrative review can be gamed. Deemed approval can’t. If the agency doesn’t act, the permit approves itself. The FCC has done this for wireless facilities since 2014. California has done it for land use since 1977.
  3. Close the backdoors. A permit shield so eagle prosecutions can’t be used as a backdoor revocation. Economic protections so tax credits can’t be eliminated after construction starts. Anti-fee provisions so permitted projects can’t be made uneconomic by tripling lease fees. Mandatory system access so developers can’t be locked out of the digital tools they need to even start the process.

The current bills protect permitted projects from political interference. This proposal makes projects unpermittable-by-obstruction — because every door has a clock, every clock has a deemed approval, and every workaround has a cause of action.

Tier 2: Hidden Choke Points (Not Covered)

These agencies hold independent statutory authorities that can independently kill or indefinitely delay an energy project. Neither bill explicitly covers them.

FAA — Federal Aviation Administration

Authority: 14 CFR Part 77; 49 U.S.C. §44718; FAA Reauthorization Act of 2024

What it controls: Every wind turbine over 200 feet requires an FAA Determination of No Hazard (DNH) via Form 7460-1. Each turbine is filed individually. FAA conducts an aeronautical study reviewing radar interference, flight paths, and air navigation. DNH determinations expire after 36 months.

How it can obstruct:

  • Issue a Determination of Hazard, killing the turbine location
  • Simply sit on the evaluation — there is no statutory deadline for FAA to complete an aeronautical study
  • Impose onerous radar mitigation requirements that are technically or financially infeasible

Already happening: In July 2025, DOT Secretary Duffy announced a 1.2-mile mandatory setback for wind turbines near highways and railroads, citing radio communication interference. The evidence basis: a single railroad industry consultant report. FAA was directed to “thoroughly evaluate” all proposed turbines. This is a new administrative requirement created from whole cloth with no statutory basis.

DOT / FHWA — Dept. of Transportation / Federal Highway Administration

Authority: 23 CFR 645 (utility accommodations); 23 CFR 658.17 (federal weight limits)

What it controls: Transmission lines crossing federal-aid highways require ROW use agreements or utility accommodation permits. Wind turbine blades (230+ feet), nacelles, and tower sections all exceed federal oversize limits (8.5 ft width, 53 ft length, 80,000 lbs) and require permits in every state traversed.

How it can obstruct: Slow-walk highway crossing approvals for transmission. Deny or impose impossible conditions on oversize load routes. Use NEPA review for minor highway ROW actions.

DOD — Department of Defense

Authority: 10 U.S.C. §183a (DOD Siting Clearinghouse); CFIUS (50 U.S.C. §4565); classified national security authorities

What it controls: Military radar/airspace compatibility reviews for all wind projects. CFIUS national security review for projects with foreign investment (Equinor, Orsted, and other major developers are foreign entities).

How it can obstruct: Claim radar interference (the exact pretext for the offshore wind stop-work orders). The Clearinghouse review has no statutory deadline and no deemed-concurrence. CFIUS can impose impossible mitigation or block the transaction entirely.

Already happening: The December 2025 suspension of all five offshore wind projects cited classified DOD radar reports. The July 2025 EO “Ending Market Distorting Subsidies for Unreliable, Foreign-Controlled Energy Sources” explicitly frames renewables as “foreign-controlled,” expanding potential CFIUS jurisdiction.

Coast Guard (DHS)

Authority: 33 CFR Parts 62, 165; Ports and Waterways Safety Act

What it controls: Every offshore wind turbine requires a separate Private Aids to Navigation (PATON) permit. The Coast Guard establishes 500-meter safety zones around each structure during construction. For CVOW (176 turbines + 3 substations), that’s 179 separate safety zones.

How it can obstruct: Slow-walk PATON applications. Expand safety zones. Issue adverse navigation safety assessments requiring project redesign.

NOAA / NMFS — National Marine Fisheries Service

Authority: MMPA (16 U.S.C. §1361); ESA §7 (marine species); Magnuson-Stevens Act (EFH)

What it controls: Offshore wind construction requires Marine Mammal Incidental Harassment Authorizations, ESA Biological Opinions for marine species (North Atlantic right whale is the critical one), and Essential Fish Habitat consultations. Each is a separate process.

How it can obstruct: NMFS has taken years on IHAs. In May 2024, NMFS requested reinitiation of consultation on Vineyard Wind mid-construction, citing new information on listed species. Seasonal construction restrictions for marine mammals can compress construction to impossible windows.

ACHP / SHPO / THPO — Historic Preservation

Authority: NHPA §106 (54 U.S.C. §306108)

What it controls: Every federal permit triggers §106 consultation on impacts to historic properties. Requires engagement with State Historic Preservation Officers, Tribal Historic Preservation Officers, Indian Tribes, and Native Hawaiian Organizations.

How it can obstruct: Section 106 consultation has no statutory completion deadline. Tribal consultation can extend indefinitely. The 2025 “National Energy Emergency” EO gave ACHP emergency 7-day comment provisions for fossil fuel development — but not for renewables.

Other Agencies

  • State Department: Presidential Permits for transmission crossing international borders (E.O. 10485). No deadline. The Keystone XL saga proved this can be weaponized in either direction.
  • Forest Service (USDA): Special use permits for projects on national forest land (~193M acres). A Burgum-style memo from the Agriculture Secretary would freeze wind/solar on the entire National Forest System.
  • Bureau of Reclamation: Land use permits on reclamation lands. Relevant for solar co-location near reservoirs and irrigation infrastructure.
  • Bureau of Indian Affairs: ROW approvals for projects crossing tribal lands (25 CFR Part 169). Currently takes 7–10 years for a transmission line crossing tribal lands.

Tier 3: Pretextual Enforcement

Even after a project has all its permits, enforcement actions under separate statutes can function as backdoor revocations. This is the “eagle law fuckery” vector.

BGEPA Weaponization

The Bald and Golden Eagle Protection Act (16 U.S.C. §668) makes it a federal crime to “take” (kill, wound, or disturb) any bald or golden eagle without a permit. Wind turbines inevitably kill some eagles. The Biden administration created expedited general permits in February 2024. The Trump administration can:

  • Revoke or not renew eagle take permits
  • Criminally prosecute operators for any eagle deaths exceeding permit parameters
  • Refuse to issue new permits, creating criminal liability for any operating wind farm
  • Use the threat of prosecution to chill new development

Already happening: In August 2025, the administration announced a crackdown on wind farm developers for eagle deaths. Prior precedent: ESI Energy/NextEra paid $8M+ for 150 eagle deaths across eight states.

MBTA Double Standard

The Migratory Bird Treaty Act (16 U.S.C. §703) prohibits “take” of migratory birds. In April 2025, Acting Solicitor Zerzan reimposed the Trump 1.0 interpretation limiting MBTA to intentional acts only — which benefits fossil operations (incidental kills from flaring, pits, power lines are not prosecuted) while the administration simultaneously targets wind through BGEPA. A regulatory double standard created by selective enforcement.

IPaC System Blocking

FWS blocked wind and solar developers from accessing the IPaC system (Information for Planning and Consultation) — the automated website developers use for species determinations. This doesn’t deny anything. It simply removes the tool that lets you even start the consultation process, forcing projects into manual review that takes months or years. It’s a pre-permitting attack that no current bill addresses.

Tier 4: Economic Kill Switches

A project can be fully permitted and still die if its economics are destroyed. The current administration has demonstrated this path comprehensively.

IRS / Treasury

IRS Notice 2025-42 (August 15, 2025): Eliminated the 5% safe harbor for establishing “beginning of construction” for tax credit purposes. Only the physical work test remains. This destroyed the financing timeline for hundreds of projects.

OBBBA (July 4, 2025): Terminated Section 45Y PTC and Section 48E ITC for wind and solar placed in service after December 31, 2027. Killed the residential solar credit (25D) as of December 31, 2025. Projects must begin construction before July 5, 2026 AND be placed in service by December 31, 2027.

FEOC restrictions: Treasury can define “foreign entity of concern” so broadly that most solar panels are ineligible for credits.

Commerce Department — Tariffs

Section 232 tariffs on steel/aluminum doubled to 50% (June 2025), hitting solar racking and wind towers. AD/CVD duties on solar cells from Cambodia, Malaysia, Thailand, and Vietnam finalized April 2025. New petitions against India, Laos, and Indonesia with alleged dumping margins up to 249%. Section 232 investigation launched into polysilicon imports (July 2025) — the basic feedstock for all solar cells. Wood Mackenzie called Section 232 “US solar’s biggest challenge.”

BLM Fee Weaponization

BLM imposed a new capacity fee for wind/solar on public lands: the greater of acreage rent or 3.9% of gross electricity revenue, replacing prior discounted rates. This is a pure economic kill that no permitting reform addresses — the permit is issued, but the project is uneconomic.

FERC Interconnection

The interconnection queue has ~2,600 GW backlogged — more than twice current installed U.S. capacity. Solar, storage, and wind are ~95% of the queue. Median time from interconnection request to operation: approximately five years. FERC is an independent agency, but its commissioners are presidential appointees. A hostile FERC could slow-walk renewable interconnection studies, reject cost allocation for transmission serving renewables, or simply deprioritize.

III. The 12-Point Framework

Each provision closes a specific category of attack. Together, they create layered defenses that an administration must defeat simultaneously — not sequentially — to obstruct a project.

1 Universal Definition of “Authorization”

Problem it solves: Both bills define “authorization” to cover permits, approvals, and certifications — but a creative administration routes obstruction through authorities not captured by that definition (FAA determinations, DOD clearinghouse reviews, IRS certifications, PATON permits).

Mechanism: Define “authorization” to explicitly include every known category, plus a catch-all:

“Authorization” means any permit, license, lease, easement, right-of-way, concurrence, consultation, determination, finding, certification, assessment, authorization, or other action by a Federal agency that is required for a covered energy project to commence, continue, or complete construction or operation — including but not limited to: FAA obstruction evaluations and Determinations of No Hazard; DOD Siting Clearinghouse reviews; Coast Guard Private Aids to Navigation permits and safety zone determinations; NOAA/NMFS incidental harassment authorizations and essential fish habitat consultations; ACHP and SHPO/THPO Section 106 concurrences; DOT/FHWA highway right-of-way use agreements; IRS and Treasury tax credit eligibility certifications and beginning-of-construction determinations; FERC interconnection approvals; BGEPA and MBTA incidental take permits; and any other Federal determination, concurrence, or authorization required for a covered energy project.

Without the catch-all, a creative administration will find the one action you didn’t name and use it as the choke point.

2 Statutory Deadlines for Every Authorization

Problem it solves: Many authorizations (FAA 7460-1, DOD Clearinghouse, NHPA §106, BIA ROW) have no statutory deadline at all. An agency can simply sit on them forever.

3 Deemed Approval for Everything

Problem it solves: The FREEDOM Act triggers contractor substitution when deadlines are missed; the CERTAIN Act triggers administrative review. Neither provides automatic approval. An agency can still obstruct by absorbing penalties or ignoring reviews.

Mechanism: If the clock expires on any authorization, it is deemed granted with standard conditions. This applies to:

  • The lead agency’s permit
  • Every cooperating agency’s concurrence or consultation
  • FAA determinations (deemed “No Hazard”)
  • DOD radar reviews (deemed “compatible”)
  • NHPA §106 (deemed “no adverse effect”)
  • CZMA consistency (existing model — 15 CFR 930)
  • IRS certifications (deemed eligible)
If a Federal agency fails to act on an authorization within the deadline established by this chapter, and the applicant has complied with all information requests within the required timelines, the authorization shall be deemed granted subject to standard conditions established by the agency for that category of authorization. The applicant shall provide written notice to the agency that the deadline has expired. The deemed-granted authorization takes effect upon delivery of such notice and has the same legal force as an affirmative agency determination.

Precedent: FCC Section 6409(a) (47 CFR §1.6100) — the self-executing deemed-granted mechanism for wireless facility modifications. California Permit Streamlining Act (Gov. Code §65956(b)) — operating since 1977 without catastrophic outcomes. CZMA deemed concurrence — already in existing law.

The Legitimate Objection — And Its Answer

Objection: Deemed approval for complex environmental reviews could approve projects with significant unanalyzed impacts.

Answer: The trigger requires: (1) a complete application (agency-certified or 6-month backstopped), (2) the applicant has responded to all information requests, (3) the delay is agency-caused, and (4) standard environmental conditions automatically attach. The agency has the full deadline period to act. Deemed approval is the consequence of agency failure, not applicant gamesmanship.

4 Permit Shield Against Pretextual Enforcement

Problem it solves: Even with permit certainty, enforcement actions under BGEPA, MBTA, or other wildlife statutes can function as backdoor revocations. The administration crackdown on eagle deaths at wind farms is the clearest example.

A project operating in compliance with all terms and conditions of authorizations issued under this chapter shall be deemed to be in compliance with all federal wildlife protection statutes — including the Bald and Golden Eagle Protection Act, the Migratory Bird Treaty Act, and the Endangered Species Act — with respect to impacts analyzed in the environmental review supporting those authorizations. No Federal agency may bring or refer a criminal or civil enforcement action against such a project for impacts within the scope of the authorizations while the project remains in compliance with their terms.

Precedent: Clean Air Act permit shield (42 U.S.C. §7661c(f)): compliance with a Title V permit “shall be deemed compliance” with the statute itself.

Critical nuance: The shield only covers impacts within the scope of the permit. If a project exceeds its authorized eagle take, enforcement remains available. This maintains environmental accountability while blocking selective prosecution.

5 Anti-Economic-Kill-Switch Provisions

Problem it solves: The administration has demonstrated that projects can be killed economically without touching their permits — through tax credit elimination, tariffs, fee increases, and FEOC definitions. No existing permitting bill addresses this.

Mechanisms:

  • Tax credit vesting: If a project has received an IRS eligibility determination or has established “beginning of construction” under an established safe harbor, subsequent changes to tax credit rules, FEOC definitions, or safe harbor thresholds do not apply retroactively. The rules in effect at the time of the determination or construction commencement govern for the life of the project.
  • Interconnection protection: FERC must complete interconnection studies within Order 2023 timelines. Missed deadlines trigger deemed approval at the most recent cost estimate.
  • Tariff stability: Projects that have entered binding contracts for components before a tariff takes effect receive an exemption for those contracted quantities.

Why this is necessary: IRS Notice 2025-42 destroyed the financing timeline for hundreds of projects. The OBBBA terminated credits for projects placed in service after 2027. The 3.9% BLM revenue fee makes many public-land projects uneconomic. None of these touch a single permit.

6 Anti-Lease-Manipulation

Problem it solves: The January 2025 OCS wind moratorium, the December 2025 lease suspensions, and BLM’s fee changes demonstrate that leases are as vulnerable as permits.

An energy lease or right-of-way grant issued under this chapter is a vested property interest. It may not be cancelled, suspended, or materially modified except upon clear and convincing evidence of material breach by the holder. Lease sales shall proceed on statutory schedules. The terms governing an issued lease — including fees, royalties, and capacity charges — may not be unilaterally altered by the lessor for the duration of the lease term.

7 Vesting Upon Application Filing

Problem it solves: An administration can change the rules while applications are pending — rescinding CEQ regulations, changing ESA listings, tightening standards — and apply the new rules retroactively to in-process applications.

Mechanism: Rights vest at the time the complete application is filed, not at the time the permit is issued. The applicant is governed by the rules in effect on their filing date. Subsequent rule changes, executive orders, secretarial memoranda, or guidance documents cannot be applied to pending applications or issued permits.

Precedent: Washington State (RCW 19.27.095) — rights vest upon filing a complete application. North Carolina (N.C.G.S. §160D-108) — a vested right “precludes any action by a local government that would change, alter, impair, prevent, diminish, or otherwise delay the development.”

Combined with Provision 1 (permit certainty post-issuance from CERTAIN Act §(a)), this creates a continuous shield from filing through operation.

8 Multi-Agency Forced Coordination with Consequences

Problem it solves: A project can be blocked by any one of 20 agencies. The FWS consultation pause froze Army Corps 404 permits (which require ESA clearance). A single slow agency holds up everything.

Mechanism (Denmark one-stop-shop model):

  • Single lead agency issues one consolidated authorization incorporating all federal requirements
  • Every cooperating agency must provide its input within the lead agency’s master timeline
  • If a cooperating agency misses its window: concurrence is presumed (CZMA deemed-concurrence model)
  • The lead agency cannot wait for a cooperating agency that has missed its window
  • Specific agencies and roles are assigned by statute: FAA completes 7460-1 reviews within 30 days of referral; DOD completes radar reviews within 90 days; NMFS completes IHAs within 90 days; FWS completes ESA consultation within the lead agency’s overall timeline

9 Anti-Circumvention Clause

Problem it solves: Every framework has gaps. A determined administration will invent new methods of obstruction not contemplated by the statute — like the DOT 1.2-mile setback, the IPaC system blocking, or the selective MBTA enforcement double standard.

No Federal agency, officer, or employee shall take any action, issue any order, directive, memorandum, guidance, or rule, or fail to take any action required by this chapter, with the purpose or effect of defeating, delaying, or impairing any right, protection, timeline, or authorization established by this chapter. Any such action or inaction is void ab initio and of no legal force or effect. A project sponsor affected by any action or inaction that violates this section may seek immediate injunctive relief in any Federal court of competent jurisdiction. The court shall award prevailing applicants reasonable attorney fees and costs.

This is the nuclear provision. It creates a cause of action against any creative workaround. If the DOT invents a setback requirement, the FAA invents a new evaluation, or FWS blocks access to a website — the developer goes directly to court with fee-shifting.

10 Private Enforcement Ecosystem

Problem it solves: Developers depend on DOJ to enforce their rights against federal agencies. Under a hostile administration, DOJ won’t sue its own agencies.

Mechanism (FOIA + citizen suit model):

  • Constructive exhaustion: When any agency misses any deadline, the applicant is deemed to have exhausted all administrative remedies and may file directly in federal court (5 U.S.C. §552 model)
  • De novo review with burden on the agency to justify the delay
  • Attorney fees and costs to prevailing applicants (creates a self-funding private enforcement bar)
  • Mandatory IG triggers:
    • Agency misses deadlines on >20% of applications in a fiscal year → automatic IG audit
    • Cumulative penalties on any single project exceed $500K → mandatory IG investigation of political direction
    • Any voluntary remand petition → IG review of justification
  • Congressional notification: Major revocations or systemic deadline failures trigger automatic notification to authorization and appropriations committees

11 Anti-Fee-Weaponization

Problem it solves: BLM’s 3.9% gross revenue fee for wind/solar on public lands is a pure economic kill disguised as routine fee-setting. No permitting reform addresses it.

No Federal agency shall impose, modify, or increase any fee, royalty, capacity charge, or financial obligation applicable to a covered energy project in a manner that discriminates between energy technologies or that has the purpose or effect of rendering previously economically viable projects commercially infeasible. Fee schedules applicable to covered energy projects at the time a lease or right-of-way grant is executed shall govern for the duration of the lease or grant, adjusted only for inflation.

12 Mandatory Access to Regulatory Infrastructure

Problem it solves: FWS blocked wind/solar developers from the IPaC system — a pre-permitting attack that prevents developers from even starting the consultation process. No current bill addresses this vector.

No Federal agency shall restrict, suspend, or limit access to any digital tool, database, or automated determination system used in connection with the authorization process for covered energy projects, except for scheduled maintenance or system security purposes not exceeding 48 hours. Any restriction in effect for more than 48 hours shall be deemed a failure to act on all pending authorizations dependent on the restricted system, triggering the deemed-approval provisions of this chapter.

This converts the IPaC-blocking tactic into a self-defeating move: block the tool, and the consultations it supports are deemed complete.

What “Airtight” Actually Means

No statute is truly invulnerable. But this framework forces a hostile administration into a choice: either violate the statute openly (triggering automatic judicial review, IG investigations, and congressional notification) or invent entirely new obstruction methods (which the anti-circumvention clause converts into immediate causes of action with fee-shifting). The cost of obstruction becomes high enough, and visible enough, that the rational calculus shifts from “block the project quietly” to “comply with the law.”

That shift — from a world where obstruction is free and invisible, to one where it is expensive and automatic — is what this framework delivers.

VI. Will This Survive the Courts?

The 12-point framework is only as strong as its ability to survive judicial review. Three Supreme Court developments since 2022 fundamentally reshape the legal terrain for permitting reform: the end of Chevron deference (Loper Bright, 2024), the Major Questions Doctrine (West Virginia v. EPA, 2022), and the nondelegation doctrine’s near-miss revival (FCC v. Consumers’ Research, 2025). Each creates both risks and opportunities.

The End of Chevron: Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo (2024)

On June 28, 2024, the Supreme Court overruled Chevron U.S.A. v. NRDC (1984) by 6–2. Courts must now exercise independent judgment on whether an agency has acted within its statutory authority, rather than deferring to the agency’s interpretation of ambiguous statutory language.

Early Post-Loper Bright Decisions

  • Seven County Infrastructure Coalition v. Eagle County (May 2025): Narrowed what agencies must analyze under NEPA — agencies need not assess impacts of “geographically separate” projects. While eliminating Chevron deference on legal questions, the Court affirmed “substantial deference” to agency NEPA factual judgments. Net effect: advances permitting reform by limiting the scope of environmental review.
  • City & County of San Francisco v. EPA (March 2025): First post-Loper Bright environmental ruling. The Court independently interpreted the CWA, holding EPA cannot impose “end-result” permit conditions — it must specify what steps permittees must take. Net effect: more specific, predictable permit conditions.
  • ESA regulatory revisions (November 2025): FWS and NMFS proposed major ESA rule changes explicitly adopting the “single, best reading” of statutory provisions, narrowing Section 7 consultation scope. Net effect: could reduce consultation-driven permitting delays.

The Paradox: Loper Bright May Actually Help Permitting Reform

Short-term risk: Every ambiguous statutory provision in NEPA, ESA, CWA, and other permitting statutes is now relitigable. Settled interpretations can be challenged. Circuit splits may emerge. The first 18 months saw Loper Bright cited in 110+ federal cases.

Long-term benefit: Under Chevron, agency interpretations could shift with each administration — what “waters of the United States” means under the CWA changed repeatedly. Under Loper Bright, once a court determines the best meaning of a statute, that meaning is locked in. This creates long-term stability. Agencies can no longer impose dramatically different readings from one administration to the next. Projects that take years to develop face less risk of mid-stream regulatory reinterpretation.

Strategic implication for the 12-point framework: The more specific Congress is in the statute, the less room there is for judicial second-guessing. If Congress defines “authorization,” “complete application,” “deemed approval,” and every other key term with precision, courts will enforce those definitions. Loper Bright rewards well-drafted statutes and punishes vague delegations. This is an argument for maximal statutory specificity in the 12-point framework.

The Major Questions Doctrine: West Virginia v. EPA (2022)

Agencies cannot claim authority on questions of “vast economic and political significance” without clear congressional authorization. The doctrine is triggered when the agency claims a previously unheralded power representing a transformative expansion of regulatory reach on a matter of extraordinary significance, without Congress having clearly spoken.

Why MQD Is Largely a Non-Issue for This Framework

The critical distinction: MQD constrains agencies, not Congress. It is a canon of statutory interpretation governing how courts read delegations from Congress to agencies. If Congress itself directly enacts permitting timelines, deemed-approval mechanisms, and federal fallback authority, MQD is largely inapplicable — because Congress is not delegating a major question, it is answering it.

  • Explicit authorization defeats MQD. The entire point of the doctrine is that Congress must speak clearly. A statute that explicitly says “if an agency fails to issue a permit within X days, the permit shall be deemed approved” IS the clear congressional statement the doctrine demands.
  • The IRA precedent. After West Virginia v. EPA, Congress passed the Inflation Reduction Act with explicit provisions designating greenhouse gases as air pollutants and directing EPA to regulate them. This was deliberately designed to provide the “clear congressional authorization” the Court demanded. The 12-point framework should follow the same approach.
  • Congress controls the terms. When Congress directly prescribes outcomes (timelines, consequences, standards), it is exercising its Article I legislative power, not delegating it.

Where MQD Risk Remains

MQD could apply in specific scenarios within the framework:

  • If the statute delegates to an agency the determination of which projects qualify for streamlined permitting, and the agency defines that term broadly
  • If vague statutory terms (e.g., “projects of national significance”) are given expansive agency definitions
  • If the federal fallback mechanism gives an agency broad discretion to define permit terms
  • Federalism amplifier: Justice Gorsuch’s West Virginia concurrence emphasized that MQD is heightened when agency action intrudes on traditional state authority. The SIP/FIP model, which explicitly preempts state permitting authority, must clearly state that Congress intends this result

The FERC test case: FERC’s Order 1920 (transmission planning reform, May 2024) was challenged on MQD grounds. Seventeen states argued it triggers the doctrine because it intrudes on state transmission siting authority. NYU’s Institute for Policy Integrity responded that FERC’s authority is neither “unheralded” nor “transformative” — FERC has regulated transmission since 1935. Lesson: the strength of an MQD defense depends on whether the claimed authority is well-rooted in statutory history. A statute creating new authority should be drafted with explicit terms.

Nondelegation: The Doctrine That Didn’t Bite

The nondelegation doctrine holds that Congress may not delegate its legislative power to the executive without an “intelligible principle” to guide the agency. Since 1935, the Supreme Court has never struck down a statute on nondelegation grounds, though multiple Justices have signaled interest in reviving the doctrine.

FCC v. Consumers’ Research (June 27, 2025)

The most important recent nondelegation case. The Court, 6–3, rejected a nondelegation challenge to the FCC’s Universal Service Fund. Justice Kagan’s majority held that the “usual nondelegation standard” — the intelligible principle test — continues to apply. Section 254 of the Telecommunications Act satisfies it because it expresses the “general policy” the FCC must pursue and the “boundaries” it cannot cross. Justice Gorsuch’s dissent (joined by Thomas and Alito) argued for a stronger test but failed to carry a majority.

Bottom line: The nondelegation doctrine remains dormant as a tool for invalidating statutes. This is good news for the 12-point framework.

Why Deemed Approval Survives Nondelegation

A deemed-approval provision is not a delegation of legislative power. Congress is not telling an agency to decide what the rules should be — it is directly prescribing a legal consequence for agency inaction. This is a conditional self-executing statute, not a delegation.

  • Congress defines when the clock starts (complete application filing)
  • Congress defines the clock duration (specific number of days)
  • Congress defines what happens when the clock expires (permit issues on stated terms)
  • Congress defines the terms of the resulting permit (by reference to statutory standards)
  • The agency retains no discretion over the trigger or consequence — it is purely ministerial

Analogies in existing law: the FCC’s §6409 deemed-granted provision for wireless infrastructure (since 2014), California’s Permit Streamlining Act (since 1977), and the Energy Permitting Reform Act’s proposed deemed-approval for LNG export applications.

Drafting Strategy: 10 Principles for Judicial Survivability

How to Draft the 12-Point Framework to Survive Loper Bright, MQD, and Nondelegation

  1. Draft with textualism in mind. Post-Loper Bright, courts use dictionaries, ordinary meaning, grammar rules, and structural cues to find the single best meaning. Every key term should be defined in the statute. Do not rely on legislative history or agency interpretation to fill gaps.
  2. Provide explicit congressional authorization for every major feature. For each of the 12 provisions, the statute should contain unmistakable authorization: timelines, triggers, deemed-approval effects, federal fallback, preemption scope.
  3. Define trigger conditions with precision. Not “if a state fails to act in a timely manner” but “if the permitting authority has not issued or denied the permit within 180 days of the date on which the applicant submitted a complete application as defined in Section X(b).”
  4. Specify the terms of deemed approvals. Options: permit issued on terms requested in application; permit issued subject to standard conditions enumerated in the statute; permit issued subject to conditions the applicant certified compliance with.
  5. Include robust congressional findings. Findings preemptively address MQD by demonstrating Congress has considered and decided the “major” questions. They provide courts with interpretive context. They satisfy clear-statement requirements.
  6. Address federalism explicitly. Clearly state that Congress intends to preempt state law in specified circumstances. Explain the necessity for federal action. Preserve state authority where possible (SIP/FIP model). This reduces the federalism “amplifier” on MQD challenges.
  7. Use the SIP/FIP structure deliberately. The Clean Air Act model has survived 50+ years of constitutional challenge. Give states first opportunity to act; federalize only on failure. This is the most constitutionally tested framework for cooperative federalism with federal backstop.
  8. Bound agency discretion with standards, not goals. Instead of “the agency shall consider environmental impacts,” specify “the agency shall evaluate the impacts listed in Section X(c)(1)–(7) using the methodology in Section X(d).”
  9. Use mandatory duties (“shall”) not permissive authority (“may”). “Shall” creates the basis for mandamus actions. “May” gives a future administration discretion to not act. Every enforcement provision should be mandatory.
  10. Include a citizen suit provision with fee-shifting. Modeled on CAA §304. Any person can sue to enforce. Attorney’s fees shifted to prevailing plaintiff. This creates decentralized enforcement independent of DOJ or any executive branch actor.

The Interconnection Queue Crisis

The nation’s interconnection queue now holds roughly twice the country’s entire installed generation capacity. This isn’t a backlog—it’s a systemic failure. FERC Order 2023 replaced serial study processes with cluster studies and raised financial commitments, but implementation results are mixed: PJM’s reformed process still takes 1.75 years for studies alone, and the average wait time from queue entry to commercial operation has doubled from under 2 years (2000–2007 projects) to over 4 years (2018–2024).

The death spiral operates as follows: when network upgrade costs force generators out of the queue, those costs are redistributed to remaining developers, triggering additional withdrawals. The Federal Siting Authority Void

Unlike natural gas pipelines (which enjoy exclusive FERC jurisdiction under the Natural Gas Act), there is no general federal siting authority for electric transmission. A line crossing five states needs five separate approvals. Any one state can kill a multi-billion-dollar interstate project.

The Failed Framework

  • EPAct 2005 §216: Authorized DOE to designate National Interest Electric Transmission Corridors (NIETCs) and gave FERC “backstop” siting authority. In 20 years: zero NIETCs completed, zero lines permitted.
  • Piedmont v. FERC (4th Cir. 2009): Held FERC backstop couldn’t apply when a state denied (vs. ignored) an application—gutting the statute’s purpose.
  • IIJA (2021) fix: Congress amended §216 to explicitly override Piedmont. But DOE still has not completed a NIETC designation (three corridors advanced to Phase 3 in December 2024).
  • FERC Order 1920: Requires 20-year-ahead proactive transmission planning. Faces 50 petitions for rehearing and active Fourth Circuit litigation.

The structural problem is unique globally: the United States is the only major economy where no entity has effective authority to approve an interstate electric transmission line.

Distributional Equity

The costs of delay are not borne equally. The lowest-income quintile pays $52 per capita in net costs from transmission delays; the highest-income quintile reaps $80 per capita in net benefits. Black and Hispanic households bear greater costs due to higher health impacts from increased particulate matter when fossil generation persists due to delayed clean energy deployment.

The Brookings Caveat

Honest analysis requires a caveat: nearly all wind and solar projects are exempt from the three most relevant federal permits. The most significant bottlenecks are often at the state and local level (community opposition, local ordinances, zoning). Federal NEPA reform primarily benefits transmission and BLM-land projects. Interconnection queue reform (FERC-level) is a separate critical bottleneck. A complete solution requires action at all three levels—federal, state, and regulatory—simultaneously.

Three Structural Fixes to Close the Most Vulnerabilities

Fix 1: PDUFA-Style User Fee Funding

Closes: Categories 3 & 4 (Personnel + Appropriations = 8 vectors)

Remove permitting from the appropriations process entirely. Industry-funded, dedicated exclusively to permitting capacity, immune to impoundment, sequestration, and appropriations riders. The FDA model (PDUFA) generates $900M annually, covering ~80% of drug review costs. Before PDUFA (1992): drug review took 30+ months. After: 10 months. NRC model recovers ~100% of regulatory costs from industry. Permitting staff funded by user fees cannot be defunded by Congress or impounded by the executive.

Fix 2: Anti-Accretion Clause + Technology Neutrality Mandate

Closes: Categories 5 & 6 (Regulatory + Land = 10 vectors)

No executive order, secretarial order, or agency memorandum may impose permitting requirements beyond those enumerated in statute. Void as applied to covered energy projects. All provisions apply identically to all energy types—no carve-outs, no “preferential treatment” provisions, no technology-specific restrictions. This blocks the DOI 69-activity elevated review memo, the DOT setback rule, and the IPaC system blocking in a single provision.

Fix 3: Tax Credit Vesting + Economic Lock-In

Closes: Category 10 (Financial/Market = 4 vectors, partially)

Filing a complete permit application locks in tax credit rates at the date of filing, regardless of subsequent legislative changes. Projects in the permitting pipeline are exempt from tariff increases on components ordered before the tariff effective date. Government performance bonds: when an agency revokes a permit or pauses a lease, it posts a bond equal to demonstrated reliance costs ($50M/week for Empire Wind, $5M+/day for CVOW). This doesn’t prevent economic attacks but makes them financially and politically visible.

The Residual Risk

Even with all three structural fixes, state coordination (Category 8) and some grid/interconnection vectors (Category 9) remain outside the realistic scope of a federal permitting bill. State AG lawsuits are protected by sovereign immunity and First Amendment petition rights. RTO/ISO operations fall under FERC’s independent regulatory authority. The 12-point framework plus these three structural additions would cover roughly 38 of 45 vectors (84%)—up from 20% (FREEDOM alone) or 24% (CERTAIN alone). The remaining 7 vectors require separate legislative or regulatory action.
